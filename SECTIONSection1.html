    Section A.1 Section 1

    Previously, we provided a number of ways to compute the coefficients in the partial fractions decomposition of a rational function. In all those instances, we assumed that a rational function had such a decomposition. The fact that there is a partial fractions decomposition is actually an algebraic result not a calculus one, but we will provide some rationale as to why this is true. Let’s start with something relatively easy to see where the ideas come from.
    🔗
    Suppose \(a,b\) are two distinct numbers. Then
    \begin{equation*} \left(x-b\right)-\left(x-a\right)=a-b \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation} \left(\frac{1}{a-b}\right)\left(x-b\right)+\left(\frac{1}{b-a}\right)\left(x-a\right)=1\tag{A.1} \end{equation}
    and finally
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{\left(\frac{1}{a-b}\right)\left(x-b\right)+\left(\frac{1}{b-a}\right)\left(x-a\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{\left(\frac{1}{a-b}\right)}{x-a}+\frac{\left(\frac{1}{b-a}\right)}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Equation (A.1) is a special case of a more general fact stated below:
    🔗
    Given two polynomials \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) with no common factors (except trivial ones such as constants), then there are polynomials \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r\left(x\right)p\left(x\right)+s\left(x\right)q\left(x\right)=1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    It should be noted that having no common non–trivial factors is the equivalent to having no common roots (real or complex). We will not prove this result here, but we will note that its derivation is exactly the same as the following number theory result (which we will not prove either).
    🔗
    Let \(p\) and \(q\) be two positive integers with \({\mathrm{gcd} (p,q) }=1\) [i.e.: \(p\) and \(q\) have no common factors, except for the trivial factor 1]. Then there exist integers \(r, s\) with
    \begin{equation*} r\cdot p+s\cdot q=1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    As an example, notice that \({\mathrm{gcd} \left(9, \ 7\right)=1 }\text{,}\) and so \(-3\cdot 9+4\cdot 7=1\text{.}\) There is a systematic way to find the integers \(r, s\) (and analogously, the polynomials \(r\left(x\right), s(x)\)) in general, but we will just use the fact that these exist for our purposes. With the above polynomial result in place, we can manipulate as we did above
    \begin{equation*} 1=r\left(x\right)p\left(x\right)+s\left(x\right)q\left(x\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{p\left(x\right)q\left(x\right)}=\frac{r\left(x\right)p\left(x\right)+s\left(x\right)q\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)q\left(x\right)}=\frac{s\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)}+\frac{r\left(x\right)}{q\left(x\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus if \(f(x)\) is any polynomial, then we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(x)}{p\left(x\right)q\left(x\right)}=\frac{f(x)s\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)}+\frac{f(x)r\left(x\right)}{q\left(x\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This says that our goal of finding a partial fractions decomposition is theoretically possible. Before we make our “educated guess” as to what the numerators will look like, we will make an observation that might make our guess even more educated and narrow our search.
    🔗
    Observation: Notice that if the degree of \(f(x)\) is less than the degree of \(p\left(x\right)q(x)\text{,}\) then we can assume that the degree of the numerators in the partial fractions will be less than the degrees of the denominators.
    🔗
    If you think about this, it makes sense. First, we should point out that the numerators in the partial fractions are not unique, but there is no sense introducing higher degree terms in the numerators that would just cancel out anyway when these fractions are combined. For example, if we had
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x-4}{\left(x-1\right)\left(x+2\right)}=\frac{ax+b}{x-1}+\frac{cx+d}{x+2} \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x-4}{\left(x-1\right)\left(x+2\right)}=\frac{\left(ax+b\right)\left(x+2\right)+(cx+d)(x-1)}{\left(x-1\right)\left(x+2\right)}=\frac{\left(a+c\right)x^2+\left(2a+b-c+d\right)x+(2b-d)}{\left(x-1\right)\left(x+2\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We might as well assume that \(a=c=0\) and try to find \(b, d\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 3x-4=\left(b+d\right)x+(2b-d) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Again, we want to point out that this is not necessary, but it makes for a more “educated” guess and cuts out some unnecessary work.
    🔗
    The same ideas can be extended if we have three or more polynomials in the denominator which pairwise have no non–trivial factors.
    🔗

    Theorem A.1.0.1. The PFD with Three Factors.

    Suppose we have three polynomials \(n\left(x\right),p\left(x\right), q\left(x\right)\) which pairwise have no non-trivial common factors. Then there exist polynomials \(r\left(x\right),s\left(x\right),t(x)\) with
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{n\left(x\right)\cdot p\left(x\right)\cdot q(x)}=\frac{r\left(x\right)}{n\left(x\right)}+\frac{s\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)}+\frac{t\left(x\right)}{q\left(x\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem A.1.0.2.

    Prove Theorem Theorem A.1.0.1.
    🔗
    Hint.
    We can write \(\frac{1}{n\left(x\right)\cdot p\left(x\right)\cdot q(x)}=\frac{r\left(x\right)}{n\left(x\right)}+\frac{m\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)\cdot q(x)}=\frac{r\left(x\right)}{n\left(x\right)}+m\left(x\right)\left(\frac{1}{p\left(x\right)\cdot q\left(x\right)}\right)\) for some polynomials \(r\left(x\right), m(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Since the goal in a partial fractions decomposition is to split a rational function into as many “simpler” pieces as possible, we will explore what can happen. We will use specific examples to illustrate these situations, but the ideas involved apply in general.
    🔗
    We already took care of the case where we have distinct linear factors. What if we have a factor which is repeated. For example, suppose we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x^3\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(x^3\) and \(3x-1\) have no non trivial common factors, then we already know we can write
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x^3\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{Ax^2+Bx+C}{x^3}+\frac{D}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    where the constants \(A,B,C,D\) need to be determined by one of the methods we employed before. Of course, this can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x^3\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x^3}+\frac{D}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    which represents our partial fractions decomposition.
    🔗
    We can employ a similar idea if we have something like \(\frac{1}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3\left(x-1\right)}\)
    🔗
    In this case we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{Ax^2+Bx+C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3}+\frac{D}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we rewrite this as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{A{[\left(x+1)-1\right]}^2+B[\left(x+1\right)-1]+C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3}+\frac{D}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{{A\left(x+1\right)}^2-2A\left(x+1\right)+A+B\left(x+1\right)-B+C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3}+\frac{D}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{-2A+B}{{\left(x+1\right)}^2}+\frac{-2A-B+C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3}+\frac{D}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we relabel things, we get our partial fractions decomposition
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{B}{{\left(x+1\right)}^2}+\frac{C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^3}+\frac{D}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    where \(A,B,C,D\) are constants that need to be determined using methods we learned before.
    🔗
    Suppose our repeated factor is not linear, say we had \(\frac{1}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2x}\text{.}\) By our general result above, we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2x}=\frac{Ax^3+Bx^2+Cx+D}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2}+\frac{E}{x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Employing a similar trick to what we did with a repeated linear factor, we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2x}=\frac{A\left(x^3+x\right)+B\left(x^2+1\right)+Cx-Ax-B+D}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2}+\frac{E}{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{Ax\left(x^2+1\right)+B\left(x^2+1\right)+\left(C-A\right)x+D-B}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2}+\frac{E}{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{Ax+B}{x^2+1}+\frac{\left(C-A\right)x+D-B}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2}+\frac{E}{x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Relabeling, we have our partial fractions decomposition
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2x}=\frac{Ax+B}{x^2+1}+\frac{Cx+D}{{\left(x^2+1\right)}^2}+\frac{E}{x} \end{equation*}
    where the coefficients \(A,B,C,D,E\) can be determined by one of our previous methods.
    🔗
    We will stop at linear and quadratic factors even though the ideas we just illustrated would work for them as well. The fact is that any polynomial of degree three or higher can be factored into two or more other polynomials of lesser degree. This says that linear and quadratic factors are the only possible irreducible factors. The reason that this is true follows by extending our roots to complex numbers as well. There are a couple of reasons for doing this.
    🔗
    First: The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, says that any polynomial \(q(x)\) with real coefficients can factored into the form \(q\left(x\right)=a_n\left(x-r_1\right)\left(x-r_2\right)\cdots \left(x-r_n\right)\) where \(r_1,r_2,\dots ,r_n\) are the (possibly complex) roots of \(q(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Second: If \(r=a+bi\) is a root of \(q(x)\) then its complex conjugate \(\overline{r}=a-bi\) is also a root.
    🔗
    When we multiply two such linear factors together, we have
    \begin{equation*} \left(x-\left(a+bi\right)\right)\left(x-\left(a-bi\right)\right)=x^2-2ax+(a^2+b^2) \end{equation*}
    which is a quadratic factor with real coefficients.
    🔗
    The upshot of all this is that if we want to find the partial fractions decomposition of a rational function \(\frac{p\left(x\right)}{q\left(x\right)}\text{,}\) we do the following:
    🔗

    Algorithm A.1.0.3. The Partial Fraction Decomposition.

    1. Use long division to ensure that the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator.
      🔗
    2. Factor the denominator completely (over the real numbers). These irreducible factors will either be linear or quadratic.
      🔗
    3. For any linear factor of the form \({\left(ax+b\right)}^n\text{,}\) form the sum
      \begin{equation*} \frac{A_1}{ax+b}+\frac{A_2}{{\left(ax+b\right)}^2}+\dots +\frac{A_n}{{\left(ax+b\right)}^n} \end{equation*}
      🔗
    4. For any quadratic factor of the form \({\left(ax^2+bx+c\right)}^m\text{,}\) form the sum
      \begin{equation*} \frac{B_1x+C_1}{ax^2+bx+c}+\frac{B_2x+C_2}{{\left(ax^2+bx+c\right)}^2}+\dots +\frac{B_mx+C_m}{{\left(ax^2+bx+c\right)}^m} \end{equation*}
      🔗
    5. Find all the coefficients using one of the methods we employed before.
      🔗
