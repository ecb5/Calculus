    Section 25.5 Radius of Convergence of a Power Series

    Here is the main theorem concerning the convergence of a power series.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.5.0.1.

    If \(b\) is a nonzero real number such that \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nb^n}\) converges, the \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\)converges absolutely for all \(x\) with \(\abs{x}\lt{}|b|\)
    🔗
    🔗
    To see this, first notice that if \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nb^n}\)converges, then \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ (a_nb^n)}=0\text{.}\) In a situation similar to
    🔗
    there exists an \(N\) and \(\beta{}\) such that \(\abs{a_nb^n}\lt{}N\) when \(n\gt{}\beta{}\text{.}\) Since there are only a finite number of terms \(|a_nb^n|\) for \(n\le \beta{}\text{,}\) then this says that the sequence \(\left(\left|a_nb^n\right|\right)\) is bounded. So, there is a number \(B>0\) with \(\left|a_nb^n\right|\lt{}B\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} \abs{a_nx^n}=\left|a_nb^n{\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)}^n\right|\le B{\left|\frac{x}{b}\right|}^n \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(\abs{x}\lt{}|b|\text{,}\) then \({\left|\frac{x}{b}\right|}^n\lt{}1\) and \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{{\left|\frac{x}{b}\right|}^n}\) is a convergent geometric series. Thus, by the comparison test \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{|a_nx^n|}\)converges.
    🔗

    Problem 25.5.0.2.

    Show that if there is a \(b\neq 0\) with \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nb^n}\) diverging, then \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\) diverges for all \(x\) with \(\abs{x}>|b|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    What would happen if there was an \(x\) with \(\abs{x}>|b|\) where the series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\) converges?]
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In light of the previous results, for a given power series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\text{,}\) we have the following possibilities:
    1. There is a positive real number \(r\) such that the series converges absolutely for \(\abs{x} r\) and diverges for \(\abs{x}>r\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. the series only converges for \(x=0\text{,}\)
      🔗
    3. the series converges absolutely for all \(x\text{,}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    In the first case, we say that the radius of convergence of the series is \(r\text{.}\) In the second case, we say that the radius of convergence is 0. In the third case, we say that the radius of convergence is \(\infty \text{.}\) In any case, the convergence of a power series is on an interval whose center is at 0.
    🔗
    A power series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n{\left(x-a\right)}^n}\) expanded about \(a\text{.}\) Has a radius of convergence also. This can be seen by utilizing the substitution \(z=x-a\text{.}\) This power series will converge absolutely for \(\abs{x-a}=\left|z\right|\lt{}r\) and diverge for \(\abs{x-a}=\left|z\right|>r\text{.}\) In this case, the interval of convergence is centered at \(a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    So, in theory, every power series has a radius of convergence \(r\) with \(0\le r\le \infty \text{.}\) However, we have not really indicated how one actually computes this. For that, we will go back to general numerical series and introduce a new test for convergence.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.5.0.3. The Ratio Test.

    Suppose we have a series \(\sum{a_n}\) whose terms are not zero. Suppose
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}}=L} \end{equation*}
    If \(L\lt{}1\) then \(\sum{a_n}\) converges absolutely.
    🔗
    If \(L\gt{}1\) then \(\sum{a_n}\) diverges.
    🔗
    🔗
    If \(L=1\) then the test is inconclusive: the series could converge or diverge.
    🔗
    Before we see why this is true, let’s see how this can be applied to finding the radius of convergence of a power series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n{\left(x-a\right)}^n}\text{.}\) Consider \(\limit{n}{ \infty} { \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}{\left(x-a\right)}^{n+1}}{a_n{\left(x-a\right)}^n}}}=|x-a|\limit{n}{ \infty} { \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|}\text{.}\) IF \(\limit{n}{ \infty} { \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|}\) exists and is equal to \(L\) (which must be \(\ge 0\)), then the power series converges absolutely if
    \begin{equation*} \abs{x-a}L\lt{}1\text{ or }\left|x-a\right|\lt{}\frac{1}{L} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    with the understanding that when \(L=0\text{,}\) then \(\frac{1}{L}=\infty \text{.}\) It also diverges when
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \abs{x-a}L>1\text{ or }\left|x-a\right|>\frac{1}{L} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This says that the radius of convergence is \(\frac{1}{L}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As an example, consider the geometric series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{x^n}\text{,}\) which we already know has a radius of convergence 1. If we applied the ratio test as above, the radius of convergence is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}}=\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \frac{1}{1}}=1}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    One of the variations we had of the geometric series was \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{{\left(2x-1\right)}^n}\text{.}\) If we were to let \(z=2x-1\text{,}\) then this would be a geometric series (in \(z\)) converging absolutely for \(\abs{2x-1}=\left|z\right|\lt{}1\text{.}\) Manipulating this we get that the series converges for
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2\abs{x-\frac{1}{2}}\lt{}1\text{ or }\left|x-\frac{1}{2}\right|\lt{}\frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    So, the radius of convergence is \(\frac{1}{2}\) and the center of the interval of convergence is also \(\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) We can obtain both of these bits of information by applying the ratio test to the original series
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{{\left(2x-1\right)}^{n+1}}{{\left(2x-1\right)}^n}}}=\limit{n}{ \infty}{ |2x-1|}=|2x-1| \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So, by the ratio test, this converges absolutely when \(\abs{2x-1}\lt{}1\) and diverges when \(|2x-1|\gt{}1\)
    🔗
    We can rewrite this as \(\abs{x-\frac{1}{2}}\lt{}\frac{1}{2}\lt{}\) and \(\left|x-\frac{1}{2}\right|>\frac{1}{2}\) so we obtain the same radius of convergence and center of convergence as before.
    🔗
    The beauty of this is that it can be applied to series which are not related to a geometric series. Consider the series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{x^n}{n!}}\text{.}\) Consider
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{\left(\frac{x^{n+1}}{\left(n+1\right)!}\right)}{\left(\frac{x^n}{n!}\right)}}}=\left|x\right|\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \frac{n!}{\left(n+1\right)!}}=\left|x\right|\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \frac{1}{n+1}}=0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This says that this series converges absolutely for all \(x\) and the radius of convergence is \(\infty \text{.}\) Of course, we defined the limit of this series to be the function \(\exp (x)\) and intimated that it is equal to \(e^x\) (or at least mentioned why it is \(e^x\) for rational values of \(x\)). We never actually proved the series converged for all \(x\) until now.
    🔗

    Problem 25.5.0.4.

    Use the ratio test to show that
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^k}{\left(2k+1\right)!}}x^{2k+1}\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^k}{(2k)!}x^{2k}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    converge absolutely for all \(x\text{.}\) Note that these are the Taylor series expansions of \(\sin (x)\)and \(\cos (x)\text{,}\) respectively. This problem does not say that these series converge to those particular functions. We will come back to this in Section XX along with issues about \(e^x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 25.5.0.5.

    We introduced the binomial series for the function \({\left(1+x\right)}^\alpha{}\) back in Section 22.2.6
    \begin{equation*} 1+\sum^{\infty }_{n=1}{\frac{\alpha{}\left(\alpha{}-1\right)\left(\alpha{}-3\right)\dots \left(\alpha{}-\left(n-1\right)\right)}{n!}}x^n \end{equation*}
    and claimed that its radius of convergence is 1 (not in so many words) when \(\alpha{}\) is not a nonnegative integer. Use the ratio test to back up this claim. Why doesn’t the ratio test work when \(\alpha{}\) is a nonnegative integer?
    🔗
    🔗

    Drill 25.5.0.7.

    Use the ratio test to find the radius of\textbf{ }convergence of the following power series.
    1. \(\displaystyle \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{nx^n}\)

    Now that we’ve seen the ratio test in use, let’s see why it is true. Suppose we have a series \(\sum{a_n}\)with nonzero terms (at least for large values of \(n\)). Suppose further that
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}}}=L\lt{}1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Choose any number \(R\) with \(L\lt{}R\lt{}1\text{.}\) Since \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}}}=L\lt{}R\) then there is some positive integer \(N\) with
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\abs{a_{n+1}}}{\left|a_n\right|}\lt{}R\text{ or }\left|a_{n+1}\right|\lt{}|a_n|R \end{equation*}
    for all \(n\ge N\text{.}\) Thus, we have
    \begin{align*} \abs{a_{N+1}}\amp{}\lt{}\left|a_N\right|R\\ \abs{a_{N+2}}\amp{}\lt{}\left|a_{N+!}\right|R\lt{}\left|a_N\right|R^2\\ \abs{a_{N+3}}\amp{}\lt{}\left|a_{N+2}\right|R\lt{}\left|a_N\right|R^3\\ \vdots\amp{}\\ \abs{a_{N+k}}\amp{}\lt{}\left|a_{N+k-1}\right|R\lt{}\left|a_N\right|R^k\\ \vdots \amp{} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since \(0\lt{}R\lt{}1\text{,}\) then \(\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{\abs{a_N}R^k}=|a_N|\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{R^k}\) is a convergent geometric series. So by the comparison test \(\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{|a_{N+k}|}\)converges and the original series \(\sum{a_n}\)converges absolutely.
    🔗

    There is another test for convergence which can be applied to find the radius of convergence of a power series.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.5.0.10. The Root Test.

    Consider the series \(\sum{a_n}\) and suppose \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}}=L.\) If \(L\lt{}1\lt{}\) then \(\sum{a_n}\) converges absolutely
    🔗
    If \(L>1\) then \(\sum{a_n}\) diverges.
    🔗
    If \(L=1\) then the test is inconclusive: the series could converge or diverge.
    🔗
    🔗
    An advantage of this test over the ratio test is that we need not assume the terms are nonzero at any point. There is another advantage of the root test over the ratio test which deals with the fact that \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \abs{\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}}}\) may not exist whereas \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}}\) does exist. We won’t prove this here, but it is a theorem that if the limit of the ratios exists, then limit of the roots exists and these limits are equal. To see an example where the root test gives a definitive answer and the ratio test will not, consider the series -->
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\left(2-{\left(-1\right)}^n\right)x^n}=1+3x+x^2+3x^3+x^4+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we look at the sequence of ratios of the absolute values, we get
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left(3\abs{x},\frac{\left|x\right|}{3},3\left|x\right|,\frac{\left|x\right|}{3},\dots \right) \end{equation*}
    and the limit of these ratios does not exist. However, if we look at the sequence of the roots, we get
    \begin{align*} {\left(2-{\left(-1\right)}^n\right)}^{\frac{1}{n}}\abs{x}=\begin{cases} 1\abs{x} \amp{} \text{when } n \text{ is even}\\ 3^{\frac{1}{n}}\abs{x} \amp{} \text{when } n \text{ is odd} \end{cases} \end{align*}
    🔗

    Problem 25.5.0.11.

    Show that \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ {\left(2-{\left(-1\right)}^n\right)}^{\frac{1}{n}}}=1\) so the radius of convergence of the series is \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Furthermore, even if the limit of the roots does not exist, there is still a formula for the radius of convergence of a power series in terms of the sequence of roots. We won’t get into that here.
    🔗
    This being said, when the limit of the ratios exists, it is often easier to work with. For example, we know by the ratio test that \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{1}{n!}x^n}\) converges absolutely for all \(x\text{.}\) If we try to apply the root test to this, we would consider
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ {\left(\frac{1}{n!}\right)}^{\frac{1}{n}}|x|\ }=|x|\limit{n}{ \infty}{ {\left(\frac{1}{n!}\right)}^{\frac{1}{n}}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Determining this limit is not an easy task given the \(n!\text{.}\) The way this is typically done is using something called Stirling’s approximation (which we will sate but not prove). Basically, this says that for large \(n\text{,}\) \(n!\) can be approximated by \(\sqrt{2\pi{}n}{\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)}^n\text{.}\) Utilizing this, we have
    \begin{gather*} \limit{n} {\infty} {\left(\frac{1}{n!}\right)^\frac{1}{n}}= \limit{n} {\infty{}} {\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi{}n}}\right)^\frac{1}{n}\left(\frac{e}{n}\right)}= \frac{1} {\sqrt{\pi{}}}\limit{n}{\infty{}} {\left(\frac{1}{2n}\right)^\frac{1}{2n}}\limit{n}{\infty{}}{\frac{e}{n}} \end{gather*}
    To get this to be zero, we need to know that \(\limit{n}{ \infty} { \left(\frac{1}{2n}\right)}^{\frac{1}{2n}}\) exists. To see that, let’s make the substitution \(t=\frac{1}{2n}\)and consider
    \begin{equation*} \rlimit{t }{0}{t^t} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Fortunately, we examined this limit in Example 12.4.2.9 and determined it to be 1. Thus
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ {\left(\frac{1}{n!}\right)}^{\frac{1}{n}}|x|}=0\lt{}1 \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) and so by the root test, the series converges absolutely for all \(x\text{.}\) As you can see, this took a lot more work than the ratio test took. The root test does have an advantage when dealing with coefficients with exponents in them.
    🔗
