    Section 25.6 Continuity, Differentiation, and Integration of Power Series

    The second issue with power series we want to handle is that of differentiating and integrating a power series term by term. Since the derivative and integral of a finite sum is the sum of derivatives and integrals, it is natural to think that power series behave the same way. This was the viewpoint of mathematicians in the 18\({}^{th}\) century who utilized power series to exploit calculus. It was recognized that power series had nuances, but differentiating and integrating them term by term was a standard technique and always produced correct results.
    🔗
    Even if you are too pragmatic to concern yourself with delving into the theory of power series, the questions concerning series are pervasive in practical problems. Consider the problem of modeling how heat flows through an object (or related to this, modeling magnetic flow through an object in an MRI). In studying the heat flow problem, Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier (1768-1839) needed a way to represent a function as an infinite series of sines and cosines rather than as a power series. For example, consider the series
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{4}{\pi{}}\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^k}{2k+1}}{\cos (\left(2k+1\right)\pi{}x)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    On the interval \((0,1)\text{,}\) this converges to the function
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)= \begin{cases} 1 \amp{} \text{if } 0\lt{}x\lt{}\frac{1}{2}\\ 0\amp{}\text{if } x=\frac{1}{2}\\ -1 \amp{}\text{if } \frac{1}{2}\lt{}x \lt{}1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Here is a graph of that function.
    🔗
    Notice that we have an (infinite) sum of continuous cosine functions converging to a discontinuous function. This doesn’t happen with finite sums and hadn’t (as yet) happened to power series. Furthermore, we differentiated power series term-by-term as though they were polynomials. If we differentiate this Fourier series term-by-term, we get
    \begin{equation*} 4\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{{\left(-1\right)}^{k+1}{\sin (\left(2k+1\right)\pi{}x)}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Here is a graph of the sum of the first forty terms of that series (truncated to fit on the page).
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.1.

    Differentiate
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)= \begin{cases} 1 \amp{} \text{if } 0\lt{}x\lt{}\frac{1}{2}\\{} 0\amp{} \text{if } x=\frac{1}{2}\\{} -1\amp{}\text{if }\frac{1}{2}\lt{}x\lt{}1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    What does this tell you about trying to differentiate a Fourier series term by term?
    🔗
    It turns out that some Fourier series can be differentiated or integrated term-by-term and obtain correct results, but others as the one above cannot. This poses the following dilemma. We differentiated and integrated powers series term-by-term as though they were finite sums. Is there some power series lurking for which this doesn’t work? Were we too cavalier in our approach in Chapter 22?
    🔗
    It turns out that the answer is luckily no. Power series are very well behaved. Given the radius of convergence \(r,\) for \(\abs{x}\lt{}r\) whatever function the power series converges to will be continuous and can be differentiated and integrated term-by-term and obtain the correct results. It took mathematicians ??? years to determine what it was that made power series behave so well as opposed to Fourier series. Delving deeply into this goes beyond the scope of a normal calculus class, but we can provide an introduction. If you are interested in reading further, you can look at the free text book \textit{A Contextual Introduction to Real Analysis: How We Got From There to Here }(URL).
    🔗
    We will deal with continuity first. It will simplify things a bit to initially consider a sequence of functions \(\left(f_n\left(x\right)\right)\) converging to a function \(f(x)\) on an interval \(I\text{.}\) To say that the sequence of functions converges means that for each \(x\in I,\) the numerical sequence \(\left(f_n\left(x\right)\right)\) converges to \(f(x)\text{.}\) That is for each \(x\in I\) and for each \(\epsilon >0\) there is a real number \(N\) such that if \(n>N\) then \(\abs{f_n\left(x\right)-f\left(x\right)}\lt{}\epsilon \text{.}\) This is called pointwise convergence.
    🔗
    By contrast, consider the following definition.
    🔗

    Definition 25.6.0.2.

    We say the sequence \((f_n)\) converges \textit{uniformly} to \(f\) on \(I\) if for each \(\epsilon >0\text{,}\) there is a real number \(N\) such that if \(n>N\) then \(\abs{f_n\left(x\right)-f\left(x\right)}\lt{}\epsilon \text{,}\) for all \(x\in I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The difference is so subtle that it is no wonder that it took mathematicians so long to see it. In pointwise convergence, an \(x\in I\) and \(\epsilon >0\) are given and an \(N\) must be determined that works for that particular \(x\) and \(\epsilon \text{.}\) In uniform convergence, the \(\epsilon \) is given and an \(N\) must be found which works uniformly for all \(x\in I.\) It turns out that this subtlety makes all the difference in the world.
    🔗
    With regard to continuity, we have the following theorem
    🔗

    Theorem 25.6.0.3.

    Suppose \(\left(f_n\right)\) is a sequence of continuous functions which converges uniformly to \(f\) on \(I.\) Then \(f\) is continuous on \(I\text{.}\) -->
    🔗
    🔗
    Before we demonstrate why this is true, notice that in our example of a Fourier series
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{4}{\pi{}}\sum^{\infty }_{k=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^k}{2k+1}}{\cos (\left(2k+1\right)\pi{}x)} \end{equation*}
    converges pointwise to
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)= \begin{cases} 1 \amp{} \text{if } 0\lt{}x\lt{}\frac{1}{2}\\{} 0\amp{} \text{if } x=\frac{1}{2}\\{} -1\amp{}\text{if }\frac{1}{2}\lt{}x\lt{}1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    on \((0,1)\) but not uniformly as the limit function is not continuous.
    🔗
    To see that the theorem is true, we choose \(c\in I\) and we need to show \(f\) is continuous at \(c\text{.}\) The idea is to replace \(f\) with one of the functions \(f_n\) which we know to be continuous at \(c\text{.}\) With this in mind, we can write
    \begin{equation*} \abs{f\left(x\right)-f\left(c\right)}=\left|f\left(x\right)-f_n\left(x\right)+f_n\left(x\right)-f_n\left(c\right)+f_n\left(c\right)-f\left(c\right)\right| \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \le \abs{f\left(x\right)-f_n\left(x\right)}+\left|f_n\left(x\right)-f_n\left(c\right)\right|+\left|f_n\left(c\right)-f\left(c\right)\right| \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we let \(\epsilon >0\text{,}\) then we can make \(\abs{f\left(x\right)-f\left(c\right)}\lt{}\epsilon \) if we can make each of the individual absolute value quantities less than \(\frac{\epsilon }{3}\text{.}\) Uniform convergence allows us to do this. With this in mind, choose \(N\) such that if \(n>N\) then \(\abs{f_n\left(x\right)-f\left(x\right)}\lt{}\frac{\epsilon }{3}\) for all \(x\in I\text{.}\) With this in mind, choose any fixed \(n>N\text{.}\) This will take care of the first and third summands above. To take care of the middle summand, we will use the continuity of \(f_n\) at \(c\text{.}\) With this in mind, there is a \(δ>0\) such that \(\abs{f_n\left(x\right)-f_n\left(c\right)}\lt{}\frac{\epsilon }{3}\) whenever \(\abs{x-c}\lt{}\delta{}\text{.}\) Thus if \(\abs{x-c}\lt{}\delta{}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \abs{f\left(x\right)-f\left(c\right)}=\left|f\left(x\right)-f_n\left(x\right)+f_n\left(x\right)-f_n\left(c\right)+f_n\left(c\right)-f\left(c\right)\right| \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \le \abs{f\left(x\right)-f_n\left(x\right)}+\left|f_n\left(x\right)-f_n\left(c\right)\right|+\left|f_n\left(c\right)-f\left(c\right)\right| \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \lt{}\frac{\epsilon }{3}+\frac{\epsilon }{3}+\frac{\epsilon }{3}=\epsilon \end{equation*}
    Thus, by definition \(f\) is continuous at \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Applying this to power series, the following result follows immediately.
    🔗

    Corollary 25.6.0.4.

    If \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\) converges uniformly to \(f(x)\) on some interval \(I\text{,}\) then \(f(x)\) is continuous on \(I\text{.}\) -->
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.5.

    Explain why this corollary follows directly from the theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    Uniform convergence is also the key to integrating a power series. With this in mind, we have the following theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.6.0.6.

    Suppose \((f_n)\) is a sequence of continuous functions converging uniformly to \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \left(\int^b_{x=a}{f_n\left(x\right)}dx\right)=\int^b_{x=a}{f\left(x\right)dx}=\int^b_{x=a}{(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ {(f}_n(x))dx}}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    In order to prove this, we need an extension of the triangle inequality for integrals
    🔗

    Lemma 25.6.0.7. Triangle Inequality for Integrals.

    Suppose \(f(x)\) is continuous on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\int^b_{x=a}{f\left(x\right)dx}}\le \int^b_{x=a}{\left|f\left(x\right)\right|}dx \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    To see why this lemma is true, notice that
    \begin{equation*} -\abs{f\left(x\right)}\le f\left(x\right)\le |f\left(x\right)| \end{equation*}
    So that
    \begin{equation*} -\int^b_{x=a}{\abs{f\left(x\right)}}dx\le \int^b_{x=a}{f\left(x\right)dx}\le \int^b_{x=a}{\left|f\left(x\right)\right|}dx \end{equation*}
    This can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\int^b_{x=a}{f\left(x\right)dx}}\le \int^b_{x=a}{\left|f\left(x\right)\right|}dx \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To see how this helps in proving the theorem, let \(\epsilon >0\text{.}\) Since \(f_n\) converges uniformly to \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{,}\) there is an \(N\)such that if \(n>N\text{,}\) then \(\abs{f_n\left(x\right)-f\left(x\right)}\lt{}\frac{\epsilon }{b-a}\text{.}\) Thus when \(n>N\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\int^b_{x=a}{f_n\left(x\right)dx}-\int^b_{x=a}{f\left(x\right)dx}}=\left|\int^b_{x=a}{\left(f_n\left(x\right)-f\left(x\right)\right)dx}\right|\le \int^b_{x=a}{\left|f_n\left(x\right)-f\left(x\right)\right|dx}\lt{}\int^b_{x=a}{\frac{\epsilon }{b-a}}dx=\epsilon \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So by definition
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \left(\int^b_{x=a}{f_n\left(x\right)}dx\right)=\int^b_{x=a}{f\left(x\right)dx}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    It turns out that this result is not guaranteed if the convergence is only pointwise.
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.8.

    Consider the sequence of functions \((f_n)\) on \([0,1]\) whose graphs are given by Show that \((f_n)\) converges to 0 pointwise on \([0,1]\text{,}\) but
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{ \infty}{ \left(\int^1_{x=0}{f_n\left(x\right)dx}\right)\neq 0=\int^1_{x=0}{\limit{n}{ \infty}{ \left(f_n\left(x\right)\right)}}dx} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    To apply the theorem to a power series, we have the following corollary.
    🔗

    Corollary 25.6.0.9.

    Suppose we have a power series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\) converging uniformly to \(f(x)\) on some interval containing \(0\) and \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} \int^x_{t=0}{f\left(t\right)dt}=\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    To see this, suppose \(0\lt{}x\text{.}\) Since the power series converges uniformly on \([0,x]\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int^x_{t=0}{f\left(t\right)dt}=\limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\int^x_{t=0}{\sum^N_{n=0}{a_nt^n}dt}\right)\ }=\limit{N}{ \infty}{ \sum^N_{n=0}{\left(a_n\int^x_{t=0}{t^n}dt\right)}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\sum^N_{n=0}{a_n\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}}\right)}=\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.10.

    Take care of the case where \(x\lt{}0\text{.}\) You can handle this by noticing that \(\int^x_{t=0}{f\left(t\right)dt}=-\int^0_{t=x}{f\left(t\right)dt}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Differentiation is a bit strange in that it depends on the uniform convergence of the differentiated series instead of the original. In general, we have the following theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.6.0.11.

    Suppose that for each \(n\text{,}\) \(f_n\) is differentiable, \(f^\prime_n\)is continuous, \((f_n)\) converges pointwise to \(f\) and \(\left(f^\prime{}_n\right)\) converges uniformly to \(g\) on an interval \(I\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is differentiable on \(I\) and \(f^\prime{}=g\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.12.

    Prove this theorem. -->
    🔗
    Hint.
    Let \(a\) be an arbitrary fixed point in \(I\) and let \(x\in I\text{.}\) By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we have
    \begin{equation*} \int^x_{t=a}{f^\prime{}_n(t)}dt=f_n\left(x\right)-f_n(a) \end{equation*}
    Take the limit of both sides and then differentiate with respect to \(x\text{.}\)]
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Applying this to power series, we have the following.
    🔗

    Corollary 25.6.0.13.

    If \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\) converges pointwise to \(f\) on an interval \(I\) and \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nnx^{n-1}}\) converges uniformly on \(I\text{,}\) then \(f^\prime{}\left(x\right)=\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nnx^{n-1}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.14.

    Show that this corollary follows directly from the theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    So, you see that uniform convergence is crucial for ensuring that power series with radius of convergence \(r\) is ``well behaved’’ on \((-r,r)\text{:}\) that is, whatever it converges to is continuous and it can be differentiated and integrated term by term. Unfortunately, it is not necessarily true that the power series converges uniformly on \((-r,r)\text{.}\) However, we have the following result that will serve our purposes.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.6.0.15.

    Suppose we have a power series \(f\left(x\right)=\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\) with radius of convergence \(r\text{.}\) Let \(0\lt{}b\lt{}r\text{.}\) Then the sequence of partial sums \(\left(\sum^N_{n=0}{a_nx^n}\right)\)converges uniformly to \(f\) on \([-b,b]\text{.}\) -->
    🔗
    🔗
    In order to prove this, we need the following lemma which is an extension of the triangle inequality (Theorem 17.4.1) for infinite series.
    🔗

    Lemma 25.6.0.16.

    Suppose \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n}\) converges, then -->
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n}}\le \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{|a_n|} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.17.

    Prove this lemma.
    🔗
    Hint.
    [Hint. We know that \(\abs{\sum^N_{n=0}{a_n}}\le \sum^N_{n=0}{\left|a_n\right|\le \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{|a_n|}}\text{.}\) Take the limit as \(N\to \infty \text{.}\)]
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Armed with this, we can prove the theorem. Since \(0\lt{}b\lt{}r\) then \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nb^n}\)converges absolutely. Let
    \begin{equation*} A=\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{{|a}_n|b^n} \end{equation*}
    Let \(\eps \gt{}0\text{.}\) Then there exists \(N_0\) such that if \(N>N_0\text{,}\) then
    \begin{gather*} \abs{f(x)-\sum_{n=0}^An_nx^n}=\abs{\sum_{n=N+1}^\infty{}a_nx^n}\le{}\sum_{n=N}^\infty{}\abs{a_nx^n}\le{}\sum_{n=N}^\infty{}\abs{a_n}b^n\lt{}\eps{}M \end{gather*}
    If \(\abs{x}\le b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{gather*} \abs{f\left(x\right)-\sum^N_{n=0} {a_nx^n}} =\abs{\sum^{\infty }_{n=N+1} {a_nx^n}}\le \sum^{\infty }_{n=N} {\abs{a_nx^n} }\le \sum^{\infty }_{n=N} {\abs{a_n}b^n}\lt{}\eps \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Since this \(N_0\) works uniformly for all \(x\in [-b,b]\) then the convergence is uniform.
    🔗
    The final piece of the puzzle in showing that we can differentiate and integrate a power series term by term for \(x\in (-r,r)\) is to show that all three series: the original, the differentiated, and the integrated have the same radius of convergence. With this in mind, let \(r_O,r_D,r_I\) denote the radii of convergence of the original, differentiated, and integrated series. If \(\abs{x}\lt{}r_O\text{,}\) then we know that the original power series converges uniformly on an interval containing \(0\) and \(x\text{.}\) Thus, the integrated series must converge (to the integral of the original series). This says that \(r_O\le r_I\text{.}\) Using the same logic with the differentiated series and the original series (which is the integrated series of the differentiated series), we would have \(r_D\le r_O\text{.}\) To finish up this piece of the puzzle, we will show that \(r_O\le r_D\text{.}\) This will say that \(r_D=r_O\text{.}\) Since the original series is the differentiated series of the integrated series, this says that \(r_O=r_I\) so all three series will have the same radius of convergence. It turns out that we can go back to the geometric series again, or in this case its differentiated series.
    🔗

    Problem 25.6.0.18.

    Use the ratio test to show that \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{nx^{n-1}}\) has a radius of convergence 1.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 25.6.0.19.

    Suppose \(|x|\lt{}r_O\text{,}\) then \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nnx^{n-1}}\) converges.
    🔗
    🔗
    To see this, choose any \(b\) with \(\abs{x}\lt{}b\lt{}r_O\text{.}\) Notice that since \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nb^n}\)converges, then \(\limit{n}{ \infty}{ a_nb^n}=0\) and so the sequence of terms \(\left(a_nb^n\right)\) is bounded, say by \(B>0\text{.}\) Thus, for all \(n\text{,}\) \(\left|a_nb^n\right|\le B\text{.}\) Thus we have
    \begin{equation*} \abs{a_nnx^{n-1}}=\left|a_nb^n\cdot \frac{1}{b}\cdot n{\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)}^{n-1}\right|\le \frac{B}{b}\left|n{\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)}^{n-1}\right|=\frac{B}{b}\left(n{\left|\frac{x}{b}\right|}^{n-1}\right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(\abs{\frac{x}{b}}\lt{}1\text{,}\) then by the previous problem, \(\frac{B}{b}\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{n{\left|\frac{x}{b}\right|}^{n-1}}\) converges, so by the comparison test \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_nnx^{n-1}}\) converges.
    🔗
    The above theorem says that for any \(x\) with \(\abs{x}\lt{}r_O\text{,}\) the differentiated series converges. This says that the radius of convergence of the differentiated series must be at least as large as \(r_O\text{.}\) Thus we have \(r_D=r_O=r_I\text{.}\)
