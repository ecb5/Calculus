Problem 19.2.1.11. The Logistic Equation, Redux.

We first encountered the Logistic Equation in Section 11.1 but at the time we were unable to find a formula for \(P(t)\text{,}\) although we were able to get a general sense of the shape of the graph of \(P(t)\) by analyzing the IVP directly. But the coordinates of any particular point on the graph (other than the initial value) were unavailable. That is, we were able to generate a “qualitative” graph of \(P(t)\) rather than a “quantitative” formula . While a qualitative graph is better than nothing, an explicit formula for \(P(t)\text{,}\) if we can find it, is far more informative. Integration provides the tool we need to find such a formula.
🔗

(a)

By separating the variables in the Logistic Equation show that we need to solve the differential equation
\begin{equation} \frac {\dx{P}} {P \left( 1-\frac{P}{100}\right)} = 0.1\dx{t}\tag{19.41} \end{equation}
🔗
🔗

(b)

Integrate the right side of equation (19.41).
🔗
🔗

(c)

Use Heaviside’s Method to show that
\begin{equation*} \int{\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)}}=\frac{1}{100} \ln\left(\frac{P-100}{P}\right)+C_2 \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

(d)

Use the results in part (b) and (c) to show that
\begin{equation*} P\left(t\right)=\frac{100}{1+Ke^{-0.1t}} \end{equation*}
where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.
🔗
🔗

(e)

Use the the initial condition \(P\left(0\right)=10\) from the Logistic Equation to show that \(K=9\) and plot this graph to confirm that it has the same general shape that we discovered in Subsection 11.1.1.
