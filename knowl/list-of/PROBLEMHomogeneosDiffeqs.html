Problem 18.2.0.8. A Homogeneous Equation.

Consider the differential equation
\begin{equation} y^2\dfdx{y}{t}=2ty+y^2.\tag{18.20} \end{equation}
As given this is not a separable equation nor is there any other obvious way to find \(y(t)\text{.}\) But with a little cleverness we can turn it into a separable equation.
🔗

(a)

Show that if \(y=ut\) then \(\dfdx{y}{t} = u+t\dfdx{u}{t}\text{.}\)
🔗
This substitution is the “cleverness” we referred to above. The rest is computation.
🔗
🔗

(b)

Use the substitution from part (a) to reduce equation (18.20) to
\begin{equation*} t\dfdx{u}{t} = u+u^2 \end{equation*}
which is separable.
🔗
🔗

(c)

Now separate the variables in the equation in part (b) and integrate to obtain
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{u(u+1)}\dx{u} =\ln\abs{t}+C \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

(d)

Confirm that
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{u(u+1)}=\frac1u-\frac{1}{u+1} \end{equation*}
and use that knowledge to compute the integral in part (c).
🔗
🔗

(e)

Finally show that
\begin{equation*} y(t)=\frac{\kappa t^2}{1-\kappa t} \end{equation*}
where \(\kappa{}\) is an arbitrary constant.
🔗
Hint.
Unlike parts (a) through (d) there is more than one step to complete part (e). Several more, in fact. Proceed carefully and check your work.
🔗
🔗
🔗
It should be clear to you that the substitution \(y=ut\) is what made this solution of equation (18.20) possible. Without that we had no place to begin. Once the substitution was made the path ahead was clear. But as you’ve just seen even when the path ahead is clear, computing the solution of a differential equation can be a tedious and painstaking process. Proceed slowly, check your work frequently, and be careful.
