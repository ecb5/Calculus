Problem 19.1.5.8.

Show that the antiderivative given are correct.
🔗

(a)

Finish computing the integral in equation (19.33) and show that it is equal to \(\inverse\sin\left( \frac{x}{2}\right) +C\)
🔗
🔗

(b)

\(\displaystyle\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{5-x^2}}\dx{x} = \inverse\sin \left( \frac{x}{\sqrt{5}} \right)+C\)
🔗
🔗

(c)

\(\displaystyle \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2-9}}\dx{x} = \ln \left( x + \sqrt{x^2-9} \right)+C\)
🔗
Hint.
If your solution doesn’t look exactly like ours don’t jump to the conclusion that you are wrong. It is possible to get two different–looking antiderivatives which are actual equivalent.
🔗
🔗
🔗

(d)

\(\displaystyle\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2-6x}}\dx{x}=\ln \left( x-3+\sqrt{x^2-6x} \right)+C\)
🔗
Hint.
This integral appears to be a completely different from the others in this problem, but it really isn’t. Complete the square on \(x^2-6x\) and then use your result in part (c).
