Example 4.3.6.1.

Suppose we wish to find \(\dx{y}\) when \(y= \frac{2-3x^2}{\sqrt{x}}\text{.}\) Rewriting this slightly we have \(y= \frac{2-3x^2}{\sqrt{x}} = x^{-\frac12}\left(2-3x^2\right) = 2x^{-\frac12}-3x^{\frac32}\text{.}\) Thus
\begin{align*} \dx{y}=\dx\left(\frac{2-3x^2}{x^{\frac12}}\right) \amp{} =\dx\left(2x^{-\frac12}-3x^{\frac32}\right)\\\\ \amp{} =\left[2\left(-\frac12x^{-\frac32}\dx{x}\right)-3\left(\frac32x^{\frac12}\dx{x}\right)\right]\\ \amp{}=\left(-x^{-\frac32}-\frac92x^{\frac12}\right)\dx{x}. \end{align*}
🔗
Now suppose we have \(z=\frac{1}{y}=\frac{\sqrt{x}}{2-3x^2}\text{.}\) Can we compute \(\dx{z}\text{?}\)
🔗

Aside: Comment.

Be sure you see why the previous approach won’t work.
🔗
There are actually two different approaches to this problem that use the Product Rule. The first is to rewrite \(z\) as \(x^{\frac12}\inverse{(2-3x^2)}\) then apply the Product Rule followed by the Power Rule.
🔗
The second approach is to clear the fraction so that \(z(2-3x^2)=\sqrt{x}\text{.}\) From here we can compute the differential of both sides (using the Product Rule on the left side) and solve the result for \(\dx{z}.\)
