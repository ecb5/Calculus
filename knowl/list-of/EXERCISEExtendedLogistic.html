Problem 19.2.1.12.

Since, as we’ve mentioned before, all mathematical models make simplifying assumptions it is important to know what the simplifications are so we know the conditions under which the model may not be realistic. For example, IVP (11.1) works fine in the short term but in the long term it predicts explosive growth which is not sustainable. We addressed this in Section 11.1 by including \(\left( 1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\) as a factor on the right side:
\begin{gather*} \dfdx{P}{t}=0.1P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right). \end{gather*}
We saw that this slight tweak forces an upper bound on the population size because it forces the derivative of \(P(t)\) to be zero at \(t=0\) and \(t=100\) (in this example), so that if \(0\le{}P(0)\le{}100 \) the \(P(t)\) will always be between zero and one hundred (why?).
🔗
However that tweak doesn’t force a lower bound on the population size and this is also unrealistic. Most populations will they tend to die out if they fall below some critical threshold. The following tweak to the logistic model:
\begin{equation} \dfdx{P}{t}=0.1P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\left(1-\frac{10}{P}\right),\tag{19.42} \end{equation}
forces a population which is too small (below ten in this example) to die out. To see this notice that when \(0\lt P \lt 10\) \(\dfdx{P}{t}\) is negative so the population is shrinking. Similarly when \(10\lt P\lt{}100 \) \(\dfdx{P}{t}\) is positive so the population is growing.
🔗
In this case, \(10\) is called the minimum viability level of the population.
🔗

\begin{equation*} P(t)= \frac{100-10Ke^{-0.09t}}{1-Ke^{-0.09t}} \end{equation*}
