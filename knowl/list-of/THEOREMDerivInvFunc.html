Theorem 14.7.0.7. The Derivative of Inverse Functions.

Suppose that
  1. \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. \(f(a)=b\text{,}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  3. \(f^\prime(a)\neq0\text{,}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  4. \(\inverse{f}\) is continuous at \(x=b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
🔗

Aside: Comment.

In fact, the continuity of \(\inverse{f}\) follows from the continuity of \(f\) at \(x=a\text{.}\) We do not have all of the tools necessary to prove this so we must include it in the assumptions of our theorem.
🔗
Then the inverse of \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=b\) and
\begin{equation*} \DD\left(\inverse{f}(b)\right)=\frac{1}{f^\prime\left(\inverse{f}(b)\right)}. \end{equation*}
