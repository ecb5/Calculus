Example 21.1.0.3. The Buffon Needle Problem.

In Examples 21.1.0.1 it was relatively simple to see how to interpret an area as some other physical quantity. Just check the units.
🔗
But there are other more abstract ways the area computations can arise. For example, in this example we will convert the abstract problem of computating a probability into the computation of the ratio of two areas. Such a conversion is helpful because, as we’ve noted before, it allows us to bring our visual intuition into play.
🔗
In 1777 Georges Louis Leclerc, Le Comte de Buffon (the Count of Buffon), presented to the Royal Academy of Science in Paris what has since become known as the Buffon Needle Problem.
🔗
Suppose that a needle is dropped randomly onto the floor where a series of parallel grid lines are drawn at a distance of one needle apart, as in the sketch below.
🔗
Leclerc had determined that the probability that a needle will land crossing on of the grid lines is exactly \(\frac{2}{\pi }\) which may seem a little counter-intuitive. How does \(\pi \) show up in a problem without a circle in it?
🔗
To keep things simple we will assume that the length of a needle is \(1\) (so we are measuring in “needle lengths” rather than inches, or centimeters, or light-years) and we’ll focus our attention on a single throw of the needle as depicted in Figure 21.1.0.7.
🔗
A few facts are clear from Figure 21.1.0.6:
Fact 1:
🔗
A throw is specified by the angle \((\theta )\) and the distance \((\delta )\) from the lower end of the needle to the next grid line. That is, each ordered pair \(\left(\theta , \delta \right)\) represents one thrown needle.
🔗
Fact 2:
🔗
A needle will cross a grid line only when \(\delta \le\sin\left(\theta \right) \text{.}\)
🔗
Fact 3:
🔗
\(0 \le\delta \le1 \) (This should be clear.)
🔗
Fact 4:
🔗
\(0\le \theta \le \pi \) (Why do we not need all of the angles from \(0\) to \(2\pi \text{?}\))
🔗
🔗
Thus each point \((\theta, \delta )\) in the \(\pi \times\delta \) rectangle (as seen in Figure 21.1.0.7 below) represents a single throw of the needle.
🔗
To see why the probability of a hit will be \(\frac{\text{area of white region}}{\text{area of the rectangle} }\) suppose we randomly choose a large number of points in our \(\pi \times\delta\) rectangle by, for example, throwing darts at it. The probability of hitting the right half of the rectangle is \(\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) of hitting the left one-third is \(\frac{1}{3}\text{,}\) of hitting the center two-fifths is \(\frac{2}{5}\text{,}\) and so on. This idea called geometric probability. In general, if you have a planar region \(R\text{,}\) whose area is \(A_1\) and a sub-region \(S\text{,}\) whose area is \(A_2\) the probabilty that a randomly chosen point in \(R\) will also be in the sub-region is the ratio: \(\frac{A_2}{A_1}\text{.}\)
🔗
The area of the entire rectangle in Figure 21.1.0.6 is clearly \(\pi \) (why?), so we can solve Buffon’s problem if we can determine the area under the graph of \(\delta =\sin\left(\theta \right)\) between \(0\) and \(\pi \text{.}\)
🔗
But this is straightforward. The area under \(\delta =\sin\left(\theta \right)\) will be the value of the definite integral
\begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{\pi }\sin\left(\theta \right)\dx{\theta } \left(=2 \text{ if Buffon was correct}\right). \end{equation*}
🔗
But why guess when we can compute? We see that
\begin{align*} \int_{0}^{\pi }\sin\left(\theta \right)\dx{\theta } \amp{}= \left[\int\sin\left(\theta\right)\dx{\theta } \right]_{\theta =0}^{\pi}\\ \amp{}= \tbeval{-\cos\left(\theta \right)}{\theta =0}{\pi }\\ \amp{}=-\left[\cos\left(\pi \right)\right] - \left[-\cos\left(0\right)\right]\\ \amp{}=2. \end{align*}
Thus the probability of a Buffon needle landing on a gridline is \(\frac{2}{\pi }\) just as Leclerc claimed.
