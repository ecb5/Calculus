Why Don’t We Distribute the Differentials Inside an Integral?

No doubt you noticed that in writing down equation (18.7) we committed one of the cardinal sins of algebra, we failed to distribute \(\dx{x}\) over the sum \(x-e^x\text{.}\) It is clear that \(\dx{x}\) multiplies the both \(x\) and \(e^x\) so it seems that the expression on the right is properly written as \(\int\left(x-e^x\right)\dx{x}\text{.}\) This is true, and good for you for noticing.
🔗
But there is really nothing to be gained by writing
\begin{equation*} \int\left(x-e^x\right)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
instead of
\begin{equation*} \int x-e^x\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since every well–formed integral begins with \(\int{}\) and ends with \(\dx(\text{something})\) it is customary to drop the extraneous parentheses.
