Problem 21.2.0.9. How Archimedes Did It.

Archimedes was able to show the results of Problem 21.2.0.7 but his methods were considerably more laborious for two reasons.
🔗
First, he obtained the result for a completely general parabola. We only showed the result for the parabola with equation \(y=x^2\text{.}\) To generalize our result would require another major computation and would take us to far afield.
🔗
Second, Archimedes did not have access to algebraic notation of any kind. Thus all of the results we obtained by manipulating formulas algebraically he would have had to interpret geometrically, which is a considerably more cumberson endeavor.
🔗
But, having obtained the results in part (a) of Problem 21.2.0.7, he was able to obtain part (c) directly.
🔗

(a)

Notice that in Figure 21.2.0.8 the lines \(\overline{AC}\) and \(\overline{BC}\) each cut off another section of the parabola.
🔗
🔗

(b)

Use your result from part (a) of Problem 21.2.0.7 to show that
\begin{equation*} \text{area of }\triangle ACF + \triangle CBE =\frac{1}{4}\text{(area of } \triangle ABC) \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

(c)

Compute the sum of the areas of the triangles constructed using the mid-points of \(\overline{AB}\text{,}\) \(\overline{FC}\text{,}\) \(\overline{CE}\text{,}\) and \(\overline{EB}\text{.}\) Then do it again. And then again, and continue ad infinitum, to show that the area of the parabolic section is equal to
\begin{equation} \triangle ABC\left[1+\frac{1}{4}+\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2+\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 + \cdots \right].\tag{21.2} \end{equation}
🔗
🔗
The sum in formula (21.2) is called a Geometric Series. You may have seen similar sums in the past. If so, and you know how to compute the value of this infinite sum, do that. You will find that it is equal to \(\frac{4}{3}\text{.}\) We will return to this question (how to compute the sum of a Geometric Series) in [provisional cross-reference: geometric series].
