Problem 21.1.0.9. Two Generalizations of Buffon’s Problem.

In the classical Buffon Needle problem the distance between the parallel lines equal to the length of the needle. We can generalize this by keeping the length of the needle equal to \(1\) and letting the length, \(L\text{,}\) between the lines vary. When we model this, we still have the following facts.
🔗
Fact 1:
🔗
As in the classical problem, the needle crosses a line only when \(\delta \le \sin (\theta )\)
🔗
Fact 2:
🔗
Each ordered pair \((\theta , \delta )\) within the rectangle \(0\le \delta \le L\text{,}\) \(0\le \theta \le \pi \) represents a throw of the needle.
🔗
Fact 3:
🔗
The probability of a hit is still the ratio of the area of the hit region within the rectangle, divided by the area of the rectangle.
🔗
🔗
It turns out that we have two different pictures depending on whether \(L\ge 1\) or \(L\lt{}1\text{.}\)
🔗

(a)

Use the sketch below to show that if \(L\ge 1\) the probability of a hit is \(\frac{2}{L\pi }\text{.}\)

(b)

Use the sketch below to show that if \(L\lt{}1\) the probability of a hit is
\begin{equation*} \frac{2\left(1-\sqrt{1-L^2}\right)+L\left(\pi -2{\inverse{\sin} \left(L\right)} \right)}{L\pi }=\frac{2}{L\pi }\left(1-\sqrt{1-L^2}\right)+\frac{2}{\pi }{\inverse{\cos} (L)\ }. \end{equation*}
