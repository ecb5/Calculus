Problem 19.1.5.1. The Pursuit Problem.

Suppose a cat \(C\text{,}\) is running along the line \(L\) in the figure at a constant speed \(v\text{.}\) When the cat reaches the point \((1,0)\text{,}\) a dog \(D\text{,}\) starts chasing the cat by running directly at it. Assuming that the speed of the dog \(\left( \dfdx{s}{t}\right) \) is \(k\) times the speed of the cat (where \(k\gt1\)) and always runs directly at the cat, find the path that the dog traverses. Such a path is called a pursuit curve. The diagram in Figure 19.1.5.2 shows the situation at time \(t\text{.}\)
🔗

(a)

Let \(s\) denote the distance the dog has traveled along its path at time \(t\text{.}\) Show that the dog’s path must satisfy the IVP
\begin{align} \dfdx{y}{x}=\frac{\frac{s}{k}-y}{1-x},\amp{}\amp{} y(0)=0.\tag{19.26} \end{align}
🔗
Hint.
You’ll need to compare a differential triangle and an ordinary triangle, neither of which is shown in the figure.
🔗
🔗
🔗

(b)

Equation (19.26) is clearly a separable equation since \(x\) and \(y\) can be separated easily. The problem is that \(s\) is also a variable. We’d like to get \(s\) in terms of either \(x\) or \(y\) but we know almost nothing about it.
🔗
But only almost. Since \(s\) is the arc–length of the dog’s path we know that \(\dx{s}\) is the hypotenuse of the differential right triangle with legs \(\dx{x}\) and \(\dx{y}\) so,
\begin{equation*} (\dx{s})^2=(\dx{x})^2+(\dx{y})^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
🔗
Differentiate both sides of equation (19.26) with respect to \(x\) and show that the dog’s path must satisfy the differential equation
\begin{equation*} (1-x)\dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=\frac{1}{k}\dfdx{s}{x} \end{equation*}
so that
\begin{equation} (1-x)\dfdxn{y}{x}{2} =\frac1k\sqrt{1+\left(\dfdx{y}{x}\right)^2}\tag{19.27} \end{equation}
🔗
Hint.
Once you’ve differentiated equation (19.26) it will probably not be at all clear how to get to equation (19.27) from there. Don’t let yourself be intimidated by notation. This is an algebra problem. Proceed carefully, one step at a time.
🔗
🔗
🔗

(c)

Because equation (19.27) involves the second derivative of \(y\) it is hard to think about. We’ll make it a little easier on our eyes with a substitution.
🔗
Show that with the substitution
\begin{equation} z=\dfdx{y}{x}\tag{19.28} \end{equation}
equation (19.27) becomes
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}=\frac1k\cdot\frac{1}{1-x}\dx{x}\tag{19.29} \end{equation}
🔗
🔗

(d)

Like IVP (19.23), equation (19.27) is a second order equation so two initial conditions are needed for a unique solution.
🔗
Explain how you know that the initial conditions
\begin{align*} y(0)=0,\amp{}\amp{}\text{ and }\amp{}\amp{}\dfdxat{y}{x}{0}=0 \end{align*}
are true.
🔗
🔗

(e)

Use one of the initial conditions from part (c) and the trigonometric substitution \(z=\tan(\theta)\) in equation (19.29) to show that
\begin{equation} z=\frac12\left(\left(1-x\right)^{\frac{-1}{k}}-\left(1-x\right)^{\frac1k}\right){}\tag{19.30} \end{equation}
is the solution of IVP (19.29).
🔗
🔗

(f)

Use equations (19.28) and (19.30) and the other initial condition from part (c) to show that the dog’s path is the graph of the function
\begin{equation*} y=\frac{k}{2}\left[\frac{(1-x)^{\frac{k+1}{k}}}{k+1}+\frac{(1-x)^{\frac{k-1}{k}}}{k-1}\right]+\frac{k}{k^2-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

(g)

What are the \((x,y)\) coordinates of the point where the dog catches the cat?
