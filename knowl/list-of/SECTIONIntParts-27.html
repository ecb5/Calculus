Problem 19.3.0.11.

(a)

Consider
\begin{equation*} \int e^x\sin(x)\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Use Integration by Parts to show that
\begin{equation*} \int e^x\sin(x)\dx{x} = -e^x\cos(x){}+\int e^x\cos(x)\dx{x}. \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

(b)

Apply integration by parts again (being careful not to switch the roles of \(u\) and \(v\)) to show that
\begin{equation*} \int e^x\sin(x)\dx{x}= -e^x\cos(x)+e^x\sin(x)-\int e^x\sin(x)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

(c)

It looks like we went full circle in part (b) and came back to where we started. But look again. Explain how we can conclude from the result in part (b) that
\begin{equation*} \int e^x\sin(x)\dx{x}= \frac{e^x}{2}(\sin(x)-\cos(x))+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗
Notice that we had to remember to add the arbitrary constant since there is no point in the calculation where it appears naturally .
