Cornerstone Problem 19.1.3.1. Sand Falling in Air and Terminal Velocity: Quadratic Drag.

In Cornerstone Problem 19.1.2.1 we computed the terminal velocity of an object falling with linear drag. Next we will consider quadratic drag.
🔗
An example of this would be a ball of mass \(M\) falling in air. In this case buoyancy is typically negligible so we will disregard it. First, let \(y(t)\) be distance the ball has fallen (so the positive \(y\) axis is pointing downward), with \(y(0)=0\) representing the initial position of the ball. As always we denote the acceleration due to gravity by \(g\text{,}\) so the weight of the ball is \(Mg\text{.}\) Quadratic drag is proportional to the square of the velocity, so \(-Rv^2\text{,}\) where \(R\) is a constant that will depend on the medium our ball is falling through (air, in this instance) and \(v=\dfdx{v}{t}\) is the velocity. For simplicity, we will assume \(v(0)=0\text{.}\)
🔗

(a)

Use Newton’s Second Law of Motion: (\(F\))\(=\)(\(M\))ass\(\cdot{}\)(\(A\))cceleration, to show that the velocity of the ball must satisfy the IVP:
\begin{align} M\dfdx{v}{t}\amp{}=Mg-Rv^2,\amp{} v(0)\amp{}=0 \tag{19.8} \end{align}
🔗
🔗

(b)

Show that the IVP in part (b) is equivalent to
\begin{align} \frac{\dx{v}}{1-\frac{R}{Mg}v^2}=g\dx{t}, \amp{}\amp{} v(0)=0.\tag{19.9} \end{align}
🔗
🔗

(c)

Use the substitution \(\sqrt{\frac{R}{Mg}}v = \sin(\theta)\) to show that the differential equation in IVP (19.9) can be re-expressed as
\begin{equation} \int\sec\left(\theta \right)\dx{\theta } = \sqrt{\frac{Rg}{M}}t+C.\tag{19.10} \end{equation}
🔗
🔗
Unfortunately we have not yet seen how to compute
\begin{equation*} \int\sec\left(\theta \right)\dx{\theta } \end{equation*}
so we can’t make any further progress on this problem until we have found an antiderivative of \(\sec\left(\theta \right)\text{.}\) We will do that next and finish our analysis of quadratic drag in problem Quadratic Drag Conclusion 19.1.3.8.
