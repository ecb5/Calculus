Cornerstone Problem 19.1.3.2. The Integral of an Arbitrary Polynomial in \(\boldsymbol{\sec(x)} \) and \(\boldsymbol{\tan{(x)}}\).

Aside: Integrating Polynomials in Secant and Tangent.

This exploration is based on the paper Integrating Polynomials in Secant and Tangent, by Jonathan P. McCammond, The American Mathematical Monthly, Nov., 1999, Vol. 106, No. 9. In his paper, McCammond shows that if \(P(s,t)\) is any polynomial in two variables then
\begin{equation*} \int P\left(\sec(x), \tan(x)\right)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
can be evaluated using the substitutions in equations (19.11), (19.12), (19.14), (19.15), (19.13), (19.16).
🔗
We begin by defining \(u\) and \(v\) by
\begin{align} u\amp{}=\sec(x)+\tan(x)\tag{19.11}\\ v\amp{}=\sec(x)-\tan(x)\tag{19.12} \end{align}
🔗

(a)

  1. Show that
    \begin{equation} uv=1.\tag{19.13} \end{equation}

    🔗
  2. Show that
    \begin{equation} \sec(x) =\frac12 (u+v)\tag{19.14} \end{equation}
    and
    \begin{equation} \tan(x) =\frac12 (u-v).\tag{19.15} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
  3. Show that
    \begin{equation} \sec(x)\dx{x} = \frac1u \dx{u} = - \frac1v \dx{v}.\tag{19.16} \end{equation}

(b) The Integral of the Secant.

Use the results in part (a) to compute
\begin{equation*} \int\sec(x)\dx{x}. \end{equation*}
🔗
The integral of the secant and cosecant functions (part (b) of Problem 19.1.3.7) appear in applications often enough that you will probably find it useful to commit them both to memory.
