Example 19.1.5.4.

If we make the substitution \(x=\tan (\theta )\text{,}\) \(\dx{x}=\sec^2 (\theta )\) in the integral \(\int \frac{1}{(1+x^2)^2}\dx{x}\) we get
\begin{align} \int \frac{1}{(1+x^2)^2}\dx{x} \amp{}= \int \frac{1}{\sec^2 (\theta )}\dx{\theta}\notag\\ \amp{}=\int \cos^2 (\theta )\dx{\theta }.\notag\\ \amp{}= \frac12 \theta +\frac14 \sin (2\theta ) +C\tag{19.31} \end{align}
You will confirm equation (19.31) in Problem 19.1.5.6. From there it is straightforward to show that if \(x=\tan (\theta )\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation} \int \frac{1}{(1+x^2)^2}\dx{x} = \int \cos^2 (\theta )\dx{\theta }\tag{19.32} \end{equation}
🔗
Now all that is left is to put everything back in terms of \(x\) which we will leave to you in Problem 19.1.5.5.
