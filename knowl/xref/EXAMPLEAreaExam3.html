Example 21.2.0.13.

In the previous example, we had a choices. If we chose to accumulate the (vertically oriented) differentials horizontally (sum in the \(x\) direction) the nature of the function forced us to split up the integral. If we chose to accumulate the (horizontally oriented) differential vertically (sum in the \(y\) direction) we needed to get everything in terms of \(y\) because \(y\) is the differential variable and the variables have to match before we can compute an integral. It’s nice to have a choice but we won’t always have that luxury.
🔗
We would like to compute the area bounded by the curves
\begin{align*} x\amp{}=0\\ y\amp{}=\sin(x)\\ y=\cos(x) \text{ and}\\ x=\frac{5\pi}{4} \end{align*}
as seen in Figure 21.2.0.14.
🔗
While in principle it is possible to use horizontally oriented differential rectangles here it should be clear that this will be much much harder than summing in the \(x\) direction. For all practical purposes there is no other choice. So, we’ll use a vertically oriented rectangles.
🔗
But notice that from \(x=0\) to \(x=\frac{\pi }{4}\) the height of our rectangles will be
\begin{equation*} \cos\left(x\right)-\sin\left(x\right) \end{equation*}
whereas from \(x=\frac{\pi }{4}\) to \(\frac{5\pi }{4}\) the height will be
\begin{equation*} \sin\left(x\right)-\cos\left(x\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus we will have to split the integral at \(x=\frac{\pi }{4}\)
\begin{equation*} \text{Total area} = \int_{x=0}^{\frac{\pi }{4}}\cos\left(x\right)-\sin\left(x\right)\dx{x} + \int_{x=\frac{\pi}{4}}^{\frac{5\pi }{4}}\sin\left(x\right)-\cos\left(x\right)\dx{x}. \end{equation*}
