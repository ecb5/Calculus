Problem 19.2.1.13. An Interesting Observation.

We saw in Section 8.13 that expanding our number system from the real to the complex numbers can provide insight into the solution of some problems. Complex numbers can also provide interesting real world interpretations of some solutions. We will explore this a little further here.
🔗
While the quadratic polynomial \(x^2+1\) can not be factored over the real numbers, it can be factored over the complex numbers:
\begin{equation*} x^2+1=(x-i)(x+i)\text{.} \end{equation*}
This means that we can compute the integral
\begin{gather*} \int{}\frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \int{}\frac{1}{(x-i)(x+i)}\dx{x} \end{gather*}
using Heaviside’s Method.
🔗

(a)

Confirm that \((x-i)(x+i) = x^2+1\) by multiplyication.
🔗
🔗

(b)

Show that \(\frac{1}{(x-i)(x+i)} = \frac{\frac{1}{2i}}{x-1}-\frac{\frac{1}{2i}}{x+i} \text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

(c)

Show that the most general antiderivative of \(\frac{1}{x^2+1}\) is
\begin{align*} \int{}\frac{1}{(x-i)(x+i)}\dx{x} \amp{}=\frac{1}{2i}\ln\left(\frac{x-i}{x+i}\right)+C. \end{align*}
🔗
🔗
Do you see why we call this problem an “interesting observation?” We’ve seen the integral \(\int{}\frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x}\) before and we already know that
\begin{gather*} \int{}\frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \inverse\tan(x). \end{gather*}
🔗
Does this mean that
\begin{equation} \inverse{\tan}(x)=\frac{1}{2i}\ln\left(\frac{x-i}{x+i}\right)+C?\tag{19.43} \end{equation}
But on the left we have real numbers and on the right we have complex numbers? How can that be true?
🔗
What do you think? Is a way to interpret equation (19.43) that makes sense? Or did we just prove that Calculus doesn’t make sense? Either way it is quite puzzling, isn’t it?
