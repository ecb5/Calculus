    Subsection A.13.1 Avoiding the Irreducible Case by Allowing Complex Numbers

    “Imaginary numbers are a fine and wonderful resource of the divine intellect, almost an amphibian between being and non–being.”
    🔗
    ―Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646—1716)
    🔗
    Traditionally a course in Calculus studiously avoids using complex and imaginary numbers for reasons that have always eluded the authors. As we saw in Section 8.13 there are times when using the complex numbers can considerably simplify the problem. This is another such situation.
    🔗
    The theorem that every polynomial can be completely factored into (possibly repeated) linear factors is known by the rather pompous name The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
    🔗
    Since this is in fact a theorem (meaning that it has been proved) an obvious question presents itself: “Why did we bother with the Irreducible Quadratic case when discussing the Partial Fraction Decomposition?” That is, why did we (the authors) tell you that \(x^2+1\) can’t be factored, when the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra says that it can?
    🔗
    Recall that in the first paragraph of the subsection Irreducible Quadratic Factors we were careful to state that “\(x^2+1\)” cannot be factored over the real numbers. This is true but it can be factored over the complex numbers (real numbers plus imaginary numbers). C. F. Gauss (1777–1855) attempted to prove the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra four times. His first attempt was in his PhD dissertation, but that proof wasn’t complete.
    🔗
    Dissatisfied with his first effort he tried three more times over the course of his life, with better results. His last proof was published fully fifty years after his first and relied heavily on the complex numbers
    🔗
    The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra is one of the results that forced mathematicians to undertake the study of the complex numbers and eventually accept them as an extension of the real numbers.
    🔗

    Problem A.13.1.1.

    (a)
    Let \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) as in Section 8.13 and show that
    \begin{equation*} x^2+1 = (x-i)(x+i)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Use the result of part (a) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \frac{i}{2}\ln \left( \frac{x+i}{x-i} \right) +C \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    Recall that we’ve already seen that \(\int \frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \inverse \tan(x) +C\text{.}\) In view of the result in part (b), do you believe that
    \begin{equation} \frac{i}{2}\ln \left( \frac{x+i}{x-i} \right) +C = \inverse \tan(x) +C\text{?}\tag{A.13} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Notice that we have not asked if this is true, only if you believe it. Hence there is no right or wrong answer to the following question: Explain why you do or don’t believe that equation (A.13). Give mathematical reasons to support your opinion either way.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example A.13.1.2.

    By allowing the use of complex numbers we can use the HCUM to compute
    \begin{align*} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{x-2}{x(x-i)(x+i)} \amp{}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{i(2i)}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{i+2}{i(-2i)}}{x+i}\\ \amp{}= \frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{-2}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{-i-2}{-2}}{x+i}\\ \amp{}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    If \(a+bi\) is a complex number then \(a-bi\) is called its conjugate. In general, the complex factors of a polynomial with real coefficients, if any exist, will always occur in conjugate pairs, so if a PFD is obtained by using complex numbers you can always hide the complex numbers by combining the terms with conjugate roots.
    🔗
    Therefore, if for any reason it is desired to remove the complex numbers from our decomposition we compute the sum \(\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}\) as follows
    \begin{equation} \frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}=\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{A.14} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Thus our decomposition over the real numbers (rather than the complex numbers is
    \begin{equation} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{A.15} \end{equation}
    Compare this with your solution to part (b) of Problem 19.2.4.1.

    DIGRESSION: How Did We Get a PFD.

    Throughout this section we’ve been citing Theorem A.12.0.2 to justify the existence of a PFD for a rational function and we’ve used a number of shortcuts to determine the coefficients involved. It turns out that Theorem A.12.0.2 can be derived from long division of polynomials, but instead of doing that, we will take the opportunity to build a PFD from the ground up though some illustrative examples and exercises. This could actually be used to compute the PFD of a given rational function in a systematic way but is not nearly as efficient as the methods employed. We are really only using it here to justify that a PFD does in fact exist for a given rational function. Some of what we have here will look familiar, but it will be included to give an entire picture of how a PFD is built starting with basics.
    🔗
    To begin, notice that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}=\frac{\left(x-b\right)-\left(x-a\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{a-b}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} \end{equation*}
    This says that if \(a\neq b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} =\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}\right) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\tag{A.16} \end{equation}
    which agrees with what we obtained in Problem A.2.0.1.
    🔗
    Earlier, we also made the claim that we can find a PFD for three or more distinct linear factors, and that this follows from Theorem A.12.0.2. We can utilize formula (A.16) obtain this as well and we can use what we already did. Suppose \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are distinct real numbers. Then
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\amp{}=\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}\right)\left(\frac{1}{x-c}\right)\\ \amp{}=\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-c\right)}-\frac{1}{\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\right) \end{align*}
    🔗
    Problem A.13.1.3.
    (a)
    Use equation (A.16) on the two fractions in the parentheses above to obtain the PFD
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)}}{x-b}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-b\right)\left(c-a\right)}}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    which is what we obtained in Problem 19.2.1.9.
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Suppose we have four distinct real numbers \(a,b,c,d\text{.}\) Using the result obtained in part a, we obtain
    \begin{align*} \amp{}\frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)\left(x-d\right)}\\ \amp{}=\left(\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)}}{x-b}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-b\right)\left(c-a\right)}}{x-c}\right)\left(\frac{1}{x-d}\right) \end{align*}
    Use equation (A.16) to show that we have the PFD
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)\left(x-d\right)}=\amp{}\\ \amp{}\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)(a-d)}}{x-a}\\ \amp{}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)\left(b-d\right)}}{x-b}\\ \amp{}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-a\right)\left(c-b\right)\left(c-d\right)}}{x-c}\\ \amp{}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)\left(d-a\right)}+\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)\left(d-b\right)}+\frac{1}{\left(c-a\right)\left(c-b\right)\left(d-c\right)}}{x-d} \end{align*}
    Compare this result to what you get using the HCUM. [You can see why we are using equation (A.16) to show the existence of a PFD, instead of using it to determine the coefficients.]
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    So far, we’ve looked at the existence of a PFD for a rational function whose numerator is one. As we indicated in [provisional cross-reference: prov], the case where the numerator is not one can be handled by factoring it out and applying the appropriate substitutions. We will utilize the following as an illustrative example, but it should be clear that this technique could be generalized.
    🔗
    Example A.13.1.4.
    Find the PFD of \(\frac{2x^2+x+1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Notice that we can write this as
    \begin{align*} \frac{2x^2+x+1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\amp{}=\left(2x^2+x+1\right)\left(\frac{1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\right)\\ \amp{} =\left(2x^2+x+1\right)\left(\frac{\frac{1}{2}}{x-4}-\frac{\frac{1}{2}}{x-2}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2x^2+x+1}{x-4}-\frac{2x^2+x+1}{x-2}\right) \end{align*}
    If we let \(y=x-4\) and \(z=x-2\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{align*} \frac{2x^2+x+1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\amp{}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2{\left(y+4\right)}^2+\left(y+4\right)+1}{y}-\frac{2{\left(z+2\right)}^2+\left(z+2\right)+1}{z}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2y^2+17y+37}{y}-\frac{2z^2+9z+11}{z}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\left(2y+17+\frac{37}{y}-2z-9-\frac{11}{z}\right)\\ \amp{}=\frac{1}{2}\left(2y-2z+8+\frac{37}{y}-\frac{11}{z}\right)\\ \amp{} =y-z+4+\frac{\frac{37}{2}}{y}-\frac{\frac{11}{2}}{z}\\ \amp{} =x-4-x+2+4+\frac{\frac{37}{2}}{x-4}-\frac{\frac{11}{2}}{x-2}\\ \amp{}=2+\frac{\frac{37}{2}}{x-4}-\frac{\frac{11}{2}}{x-2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Problem A.13.1.5.

    As we said, this technique can be generalized to any rational function whose denominator is a product of distinct linear factors. It can also be used if there is a repeated linear factor. Again, it suffices to do this where the numerator is one.
    🔗
    Example A.13.1.6.
    Let \(a,b\) be distinct real numbers. Then
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{{\left(x-a\right)}^2\left(x-b\right)}\amp{}=\frac{1}{x-a}\left(\frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{x-a}\left(\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}\right)\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{{\left(x-a\right)}^2}-\frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{{\left(x-a\right)}^2}-\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}\right)\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{{\left(x-a\right)}^2}-\frac{\frac{1}{{\left(a-b\right)}^2}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{{\left(a-b\right)}^2}}{x-b} \end{align*}

    Again, don’t try to memorize such a formula. The purpose of this is to show that such a PFD exists. There are more efficient ways to compute the coefficients as you have seen.

    We’ve taken care of the existence of a PFD where the denominator is a product of (possibly repeated) linear factors. What about an irreducible quadratic factor? As we saw before, if we utilize complex numbers, then these can be factored into linear factors as well. The beauty is that in the end, we will have real numbers as our coefficients. To see this, we need to look a little into complex number arithmetic. Perhaps even more surprising is that all this hinges on the notion of a complex conjugate. Given a complex number \(z=a+bi\text{,}\) its complex conjugate is given by \(\bar{z}=a-bi\text{.}\) The following are facts about complex conjugates.
    🔗
    Theorem A.13.1.8. Properties of the Complex Conjugate.
    Let \(z=a+bi\) and \(w=c+di\text{,}\) then
    1. \(\displaystyle \overline{z+w}=\bar{z}+\bar{w}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \overline{z\cdot w}=\bar{z}\cdot \bar{w}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(z+\bar{z}=2a\) which is a real number
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle z-\bar{z}=2bi\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(z\cdot \bar{z}=a^2+b^2\) which is a nonnegative real number.
      🔗
      🔗

    Problem A.13.1.9.

    🔗
    As was said previously, the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra says that every nonconstant polynomial with real coefficients has at least one root which could be a complex number. A consequence of this is that any nonconstant polynomial and be factored entirely into linear factors where some of the factors may involve complex roots.
    🔗
    Specifically, if
    \begin{equation*} p\left(x\right)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\dots +a_nx^n \end{equation*}
    is a real polynomial with roots \(r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n\text{,}\) (some roots possibly repeated, some possibly complex), then
    \begin{equation*} p\left(x\right)=a_n\left(x-r_1\right)\left(x-r_2\right)\dots (x-r_n)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The interesting thing about \(p\left(x\right)\) is that if it has a root which is a complex number \(z\) then \(\bar{z}\)is also a root.
    🔗

    🔗

If one of the roots of this polynomial is real, say \(r\text{,}\) then we can write \(p\left(x\right)=\left(x-r\right)q(x)\) for some polynomial \(q(x)\) whose degree is at least two. This says \(p\left(x\right)\ \)is not irreducible. If \(p(x)\) has a complex root \(z\text{,}\) then \(\bar{z}\ \)is also a root, and we can write \(p\left(x\right)=\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)q(x)\) where \(q\left(x\right)\ \)has degree at least one.
    🔗
    Problem A.13.1.11.

    🔗

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x^2+ax+b\right)\left(x-c\right)} \end{equation*}
    where \(x^2+ax+b\) is an irreducible real polynomial and \(c\) is a real number. If we factor \(x^2+ax+b\) over the complex numbers, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x^2+ax+b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{1}{\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)\left(x-c\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that everything we’ve done so far works over the complex numbers, so utilizing
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)}}{x-b}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-b\right)\left(c-a\right)}}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{\left(z-\bar{z}\right)\left(z-c\right)}}{x-z}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(\bar{z}-z\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}}{x-\bar{z}}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-\bar{z}\right)\left(c-z\right)}}{x-c} \end{equation*}

    As we’ve mentioned before, all mathematical models are simplifications of reality, and typically are modified to take into account more complexities. For example, consider the following modification of the logistic model above
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}=.01y\left(1-\frac{y}{100}\right)\left(\frac{y}{10}-1\right)=0.00001y(100-y)(y-10) \end{equation*}
    In this case, \(10\) is called the minimum viability level of the population. (Why?) Solving this would require that we compute the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{\dx{y}}{y(100-y)(y-10)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This form of this integral does not suggest any obvious trigonometric substitution so we will need a new idea.
    🔗
    The next technique is wholly algebraic in nature. The trick to try and separate the integrand into partial fractions each of which will be easier to integrate than the original. For example, consider the expression \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}\text{.}\) With begin by making a reasonable, educated guess that such a fraction can be written as \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}=\frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}\text{,}\) where \(A\) and \(B\) are constant to be determined. We can see if this guess pans out by combining the partial fractions to obtain the original.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}=\frac{A(100-y)+By}{y(100-y)}=\frac{100A+(B-A)y}{y(100-y)} \end{equation*}
    Comparing this with \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}\) we see that if they are to be equal we must have \(100A=1\) and \(B-A=0\text{.}\) Therefore \(A=B=\frac{1}{100}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y(100-y)}=\frac{1/100}{y}+\frac{1/100}{100-y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Substituting into the original integral we see that
    \begin{align*} \int\frac{\dx{y}}{y(100-y)}\amp =\int\frac{1/100}{y}\dx{y}+\int\frac{1/100}{100-y}\dx{y}\\ \amp =\frac{1}{100}\left(\int\frac{\dx{y}}{y}+\int\frac{\dx{y}}{100-y}\right) \end{align*}
    and so
    \begin{equation} \int\frac{\dx{y}}{y(100-y)}=\frac{1}{100}\left(\ln(y)-\ln(100-y)\right)+C\tag{A.17} \end{equation}
    🔗

    The Partial Fraction Decomposition Now that we’ve seen that decomposing a ratio of polynomials (these are called rational functions) into a sum of partial fractions is possible, we’ll look for a systematic way of doing this for any rational function. Our method will hinge on the following theoretical, algebraic fact.
    🔗
    Suppose we have two polynomials \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) which have no common factors (other than constants). Then there are polynomials \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} r(x)\cdot{}p(x)+s(x)\cdot{}q(x)=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example A.13.2.2.

    For example notice that if \(a\) and \(b\) are constants then \(x+a\) and \(x+b\) have no common factors. Since \((x+a)-(x+b)=a-b\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{a-b}(x+a)+\frac{-1}{a-b}(x+b)=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Aside: Comment.
    Notice that since \(\frac{1}{a-b}\) and \(\frac{-1}{a-b}\) are constants they are polynomials of degree zero.
    🔗
    🔗
    Our approach to the problem in example A.13.2.2 was somewhat ad–hoc. We will eventualy need a more systematic approach but it will be useful to look at some more examples first. So for now we will use an (educated) guess and check method. Notice that in the example A.13.2.2 we had
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{(x+a)(x+b)}\amp =\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}(x+a)+\frac{-1}{a-b}(x+b)}{(x+a)(x+b)}\\ \amp = \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x+b}+\frac{\frac{-1}{a-b}}{x+a}\\ \amp =\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x+b}-\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x+a} \end{align*}
    🔗
    It is generally true that if \(r(x)\cdot p(x+s(x)\cdot q(x)=1\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}=\frac{r(x)\cdot{}p(x)+s(x)\cdot{}q(x)}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}= \frac{s(x)}{p(x)}+\frac{r(x)}{q(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying by \(t(x)\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{t(x)}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}= \frac{t(x)\cdot s(x)}{p(x)}+\frac{t(x)\cdot{}r(x)}{q(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Setting \(A(x)=t(x)\cdot{}s(x)\) and \(B(x)=t(x)\cdot r(x)\) gives
    \begin{gather*} \frac{t(x)}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}= \frac{A(x)}{p(x)}+\frac{B(x)}{q(x)}\text{.} \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Everything we’ve just shown assumes that if \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are known then \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) can be found so that \(r(x)\cdot p(x)+ s(x)\cdot q(x)=1\text{.}\) For arbitrary functions this is a very restrictive condition. However as we observed above, if \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are polynomials having only trivial common factors then the existence of \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) is guaranteed. Thus for polynomials the Partial Fraction Decomposition is also guaranteed.
    🔗

    Aside: Mathematical Terminology.

    The word “trivial” is very context dependent in mathematics. In this context a “trivial common factor” would be a constant. For example, \(2\) is a trivial common factor of \((2x-4)\) and \(2x+6\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We formalize this in the following theorem.
    🔗
    As stated in Theorem A.12.0.2 the PFD is very comprehensive. We will
    🔗
    To extend this idea further, if \(p\left(x\right),\ q\left(x\right),\ r(x)\) have no nontrivial factors, then there are polynomials \(A\left(x\right),\ B\left(x\right),\ C(x)\) with
    \begin{equation*} \frac{t(x)}{p\left(x\right)\cdot q\left(x\right)\cdot r(x)}=\frac{A\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)}+\frac{B\left(x\right)}{q\left(x\right)}+\frac{C\left(x\right)}{r\left(x\right)} \end{equation*}
    and this can be extended to any number of factors in the denominator. Again, this can be proven theoretically, and this partial fractions decomposition can be obtained systematically, but we will adopt a guess and check method to find \(A,\ B,\ C\text{.}\) To make our guess a little more educated, the following fact can be employed. You can make peace with this in your own closet.
    🔗
    If the degree of \(t(x)\) is less than the degree of \(p(x)\cdot q(x)\cdot r(x)\text{,}\) then \(A,B,C\) can be chosen with \(\deg( A)\lt \deg(p), \deg(B)\lt \deg(q), \deg(C)\lt \deg(r)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example A.13.2.3.

    Recall we had
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{dy}{y\left(100-y\right)(y-10)}} \end{equation*}
    To compute this we will find the partial fractions decomposition of \(\frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}\text{.}\) We make the educated guess
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}+\frac{C}{y-10} \end{equation*}
    and determine what \(A,B,C\) are. This can be done in a number of ways, but the most direct (and labor intensive) way to do this is to combine the terms in the right-hand side and compare coefficients.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{A\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)+By\left(y-10\right)+Cy\left(100-y\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{A\left(-y^2+110y-100\right)+B\left(y^2-10y\right)+C\left(100y-y^2\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(-A+B-C\right)y^2+\left(110A-10B+100C\right)y+\left(-1000A\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the fractions must be equal and the denominators are the same, the numerators must also be equal. Thus
    \begin{align*} -A+B-C& =0\\ 110A-10B+100C& =0\\ -1000A& =1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Solve these equations anyway you wish, but you should get \(A=-\frac{1}{1000}\text{,}\) \(B=\frac{1}{9000}\text{,}\) and \(C=\frac{1}{900}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{-1/1000}{y}+\frac{1/9000}{100-y}+\frac{1/900}{y-10} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{dy}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}}=-\frac{1}{1000}\int{\frac{dy}{y}}+\frac{1}{9000}\int{\frac{dy}{100-y}}+\frac{1}{900}\int{\frac{dy}{y-10}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-\frac{1}{1000}{\mathrm{ln} y\ }-\frac{1}{9000}{\mathrm{ln} \left(100-y\right)\ }+\frac{1}{900}{\mathrm{ln} \left(y-10\right)\ }+c \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The PFD is Algebra, not Calculus, but as the problems and examples above show it can be very tedious algebra. There are shortcuts to the process which can eliminate some of the tedium, but the problem with shorcuts is that if you do not thoroughly understand the underlying goal it is all too easy to mess up the computation. As a rule, you should not start using computational shortcuts until you have thoroughly mastered the full calculation.

    Example A.13.2.4. Heaviside’s Cover-up Method.

    Suppose we wish to find the partial fractions decomposition of \(\frac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x-2)(x-5)}\text{.}\) As before, we have
    \begin{equation} \frac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x-2)(x-5)}=\ \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x-2}+\frac{C}{x-5} \text{.}\tag{A.18} \end{equation}
    This leads to
    \begin{equation*} 3x+1=A\left(x-2\right)\left(x-5\right)+B\left(x-1\right)\left(x-5\right)+C(x-1)(x-2) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Observe that (A.18) will be true for every real (or complex) value of \(x\text{.}\) The factor \(x-1\) appears in two of the terms on the right suggests that we let \(x=1.\) In that case we have
    \begin{equation*} 3\left(1\right)+1=A\left(1-2\right)\left(1-5\right) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3\left(1\right)+1}{\left(1-2\right)\left(1-5\right)}=A. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Aside: Comment.
    Actually we are replacing \(x-1\) with 1.
    🔗
    We did not simplify this last formula in order to highlight the following\(.\) Observe that we get the same result if we simply “cover up” the factor \((x-1)\) on the left side of (A.18) above and set \(x=1.\)
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{3\left(1\right)+1}{(\text{covered})\left(1-2\right)\left(1-5\right)}=1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In precisely the same fashion (setting \(x=2\) and \(x=3\)) we get:
    \begin{equation*} B=\frac{\left(3\right)2+1}{\left(2-1\right)\left(\text{covered}\right)\left(2-5\right)}=\ \frac{-7}{3}, \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} C=\frac{\left(3\right)5+1}{(5-1)(5-2)(\text{covered})}=\frac{4}{3}, \end{equation*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x-2)(x-5)}=\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{-7/3}{x-2}+\frac{4/3}{x-5}.\ \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Aside: Warning!
    In order to use Heaviside’s Cover–Up Method you must have distinct linear factors in the denominator.
    🔗
    In this example they were \(x-1\text{,}\) \(x-2\text{,}\) and \(x-5\text{.}\) If our rational function had been
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+1}{(x-1)^2(x-2)(x-5)} \end{equation*}
    Heaviside’s Method would not work because \((x-1)^2\) is not linear.
    🔗
    Try it and see what goes wrong.
    🔗
    This trick is known as Heaviside’s Cover-Up Method (HCUM). It is well known, and can be used to find the PFD of a rational function as long as the denominator can be factored into distinct linear factors.
    🔗
    🔗
    In the previous partial fractions decompositions, we started with a rational function where the degree of the numerator was less than the degree of the denominator and where the factors in the denominator were linear. In this section, we will expand our techniques to encompass other situations.
    🔗
    For example, suppose the degree of the numerator is not less than the degree of the denominator. If this is the case, then we can perform long division first and then find the partial fractions decomposition of the remainder. For example, suppose we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^3-2x+1}{x^2+x-6}=\frac{x^3-2x+1}{\left(x-2\right)\left(x+3\right)} \end{equation*}
    We first perform long division of the original rational function.
    \begin{align} \frac{x^3-2x+1}{x^2+x-6}& =x-1+\frac{5x-5}{x^2+x-6}\notag\\ &=x-1+5\left(\frac{x-1}{\left(x-2\right)\left(x+3\right)}\right)\tag{A.19} \end{align}
    🔗
    We can now perform a partial fractions decomposition on the remaining fraction.

    Example A.13.2.6. The PFD With Nonlinear Factors in the Denominator.

    What about something like
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    Since this function satisfies all of the conditions of Theorem A.12.0.2 we can still decompose it into partial fractions. However since \(x^2\) is not linear we can’t use Heaviside’s Method.
    🔗
    Recall that in the decomposition, the numerators in the individual terms can be chosen so that the degrees are less than the degrees in the denominators. This being said, we make the following educated guess as to the form of the partial fractions decomposition.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can now recombine this to determine \(A,B,\) and \(C\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x-1}=\frac{\left(Ax+B\right)\left(x-1\right)+Cx^2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{Ax^2+Bx-Ax-B+Cx^2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{\left(A+C\right)x^2+\left(-A+B\right)x-B}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    This leads to
    \begin{equation*} A+C=0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -A+B=3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -B=2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving this (any way you can) we get
    \begin{equation*} A=-5,\ B=-2,\text{ and } C=5 \end{equation*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{-5x-2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we wanted to integrate this, we would obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}}\ \dx{x}=\int{\left(\frac{-5x-2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x-1}\right)\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\left(-\frac{5}{x}-\frac{2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x-1}\right)\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-5{\mathrm{ln} x\ }+2x^{-1}+5{\mathrm{ln} \left(x-1\right)\ }+c \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Notice in this example that we really needed to look at the most general possible polynomial of degree one less than the denominators in the partial fractions decomposition; we would not have obtained the correct answer otherwise.

    Example A.13.2.8.

    Suppose we need to compute
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2(x^2+1)}}\ \dx{x} \end{equation*}
    Applying our partial fractions decomposition, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}+\frac{Cx+D}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(Ax+B\right)\left(x^2+1\right)+\left(Cx+D\right){\left(x-2\right)}^2}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(A+C\right)x^3+\left(B-4C-D\right)x^2+\left(A+4C+4D\right)x+\left(B-4D\right)}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This leads to the equations
    \begin{align*} A+C\amp{}=0\\ B-4C-D\amp{}=0\\ A+4C+4D\amp{}=0\\ B-4D\amp{}=25 \end{align*}
    Solving this (any way you can), we get \(A=-4\text{,}\) \(B=13\text{,}\) \(C=4\text{,}\) and \(D=-3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Getting back to the integral, we have
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)}}\ \dx{x}=\int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}+\int{\frac{4x-3}{x^2+1}\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}+4\int{\frac{x}{x^2+1}\dx{x}}-3\int{\frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}+2{\mathrm{ln} \left(x^2+1\right)\ }-3{\mathrm{arctan} x\ }+c \end{equation*}
    Notice that the last two integrals were pretty straightforward. This first integral is more problematic. There is a little algebraic trick which will allow us to break up that integral even further.
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}=\int{\frac{-4\left(\left(x-2\right)+2\right)+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4\left(x-2\right)-8+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4\left(x-2\right)+5}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-4\int{\frac{1}{x-2}\ \dx{x}}+5\int{\frac{1}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-4{\mathrm{ln} \left(x-2\right)\ }-5{\left(x-2\right)}^{-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Putting this all together, we finally have
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)}}\ \dx{x}=-4{\mathrm{ln} \left(x-2\right)\ }-5{\left(x-2\right)}^{-1}+2{\mathrm{ln} \left(x^2+1\right)\ }-3{\mathrm{arctan} x\ }+c \end{equation*}
    The trick that we employed above is something you might want to remember. It allows us to take our partial fractions decomposition a bit further. For example, if we start with
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{x^3}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2}}\ \dx{x} \end{equation*}
    we could rewrite this as
    \begin{align*} \int{\frac{x^3}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2} \dx{x}}&=\int{\frac{\frac{1}{2}x\left(2x^2\right)}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2} \dx{x}}\\ &=\int{\frac{\frac{1}{2}x\left(2x^2+1-1\right)}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2} \dx{x}}\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\int{\frac{x}{2x^2+1}\ \dx{x}}-\frac{1}{2}\int{\frac{x}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2}}\ \dx{x} \end{align*}
