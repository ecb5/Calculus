    Section 19.1 Integration by Substitution

    Subsection 19.1.1 Some Introductory Examples

    Entry #7 of Table 18.1.0.4 tells us only that \(\int\cos(x){\dx{x} = \sin(x)+C }\text{.}\) But with a little cleverness we can use it to evaluate other integrals as well. “Cleverness” comes in many forms but as we’ve seen one of the simplest ways to be clever is to make our formulas “easier on the eyes” with a substitution.
    🔗

    Drill 19.1.1.1.

    Use the substitution \(y=5x\) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int \cos(5x) \dx{x} = \frac15\sin(5x)+C \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Applying this notion to separable differential equations, we know enough integration techniques to solve an equation such as
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{y}}{\dx{x}}=\left(1+x\right)y\tag{19.1} \end{equation}
    🔗

    Problem 19.1.1.2.

    Find the most general solution of equation (19.1).
    🔗
    🔗
    What if we “complicate” this equation a bit to
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=(1+x)(1+y) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we separate variables and integrate we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{1}{1+y}\dx{y}}=\int{\left(1+x\right)\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    The integral on the left-hand side does not appear in Table 18.1.0.4, but if we make the substitution \(z=1+y\text{,}\) then \(dz=dy\) and the above equation is transformed into
    \begin{equation} \int{\frac{1}{z}dz}=\int{\left(1+x\right)dx}\tag{19.2} \end{equation}
    which we can solve.
    🔗

    Problem 19.1.1.3.

    Solve equation (19.2) and determine \(z\) in terms of \(x\text{.}\) Use your result to solve the original differential equation.

    Subsection 19.1.2 Extending Galileo’s Results: Air Resistance

    When he began his investigations into falling bodies Galileo kept things simple for himself by ignoring the air resistance. In Section 5.4 we followed Galileo’s example and assumed that the resistance to the motion of a falling body due the Earth’s atmosphere was zero (or at least negligible).
    🔗
    With an eye toward generalizing this model to account for resistance, we will re-examine this model utilizing our integration notation. With that in mind, recall that when dropping a ball and ignoring air resistance, the ball accelerates at a constant rate of \(9.8 \frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}^2}\text{.}\) If we let \(v(t)\) denote the velocity at time \(t\text{,}\) then this translates into
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{v}{t}=-9.8 \end{equation*}
    Note that we are assuming the positive axis points up.
    🔗

    Problem 19.1.2.1.

    Separate the variables in this differential equation to show that
    \begin{equation*} v=\int{-9.8 \dx{t}}=-9.8t+C \end{equation*}
    for some constant \(C.\) Show that in this case, \(C=v_0\text{,}\) the initial velocity of the ball.
    🔗
    🔗
    Thus if \(h(t)\) denotes the height of the ball at time \(t\text{,}\) then we have the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{h}{t}=v=-9.8t+v_0 \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Problem 19.1.2.2.

    Separate the variables in this differential equation to obtain
    \begin{equation*} h=-4.9t^2+v_0t+C \end{equation*}
    for some constant \(C\text{.}\) Show that in this case, \(C=h_0\) the initial height of the ball.
    🔗
    🔗
    The result of Problem 19.1.2.2 is essentially the same as the content of Section 5.4. But recall that in that section we explicitly chose to ignore any resistive forces. We repeated the problem here both to remind you of the result and so that we could couch it in the language of differential equations. That will allow us to extend this to a model in a manner which takes resistance into account. In the real world acceleration is not constant because gravity is not the only force in play.
    🔗
    In general, the faster the body is moving through a fluid (like air or water) the more the fluid tends to resist the motion. You are quite familiar with this. If you hold your hand out of a car window while the car is moving you’ll notice that the faster the car moves, the more force you will feel on your hand. Similarly when you are swimming, the harder you pump your hands and feet through the water the more force you will feel resisting the motion. These are examples of the resistive force or drag.
    🔗
    There are many ways that the drag can manifest. Some of the variables that affect the resistance of a body moving through a fluid are the velocity of the body, the viscosity of the fluid, the shape and size of the body, and the turbulence of the fluid.
    🔗
    When the object is relatively small and the medium is highly viscous the assumption that the drag is proportional to the velocity of the object makes for a reasonably accurate model. This is called linear drag. An example of this is a grain of sand falling in water.
    🔗
    When the object is relatively large and the viscosity of the medium is relatively low the drag is best modeled by assuming that it is proportional to the square of the velocity of the object. This is called quadratic drag, and would be the model to use for a baseball falling in the air.
    🔗
    Of course to get a really accurate picture, we could use both a linear and a quadratic term to represent the drag, but to keep things as simple as possible for now we’ll assume that the drag is linear. We can always adjust this later if the need arises.
    🔗

    Cornerstone Problem 19.1.2.1. Linear Drag and Terminal Velocity: Sand Falling in Water.

    From the diagram below we see that there are three forces acting on the sand particle falling through water,
    1. the weight of the particle (downward),
      🔗
      🔗
    2. the drag that the water imposes on it (upward), and
      🔗
      🔗
    3. its buoyancy (upward).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The total force acting on the grain will be equal to the sum of those three forces. Thus,
    \begin{equation} m\dfdx{v}{t} = \text{weight } + \text{boyancy} + \text{drag}\tag{19.3} \end{equation}
    where \(v\) is the velocity of the grain, and \(t\) is time.
    🔗
    Because the positive \(y\) axis points downward \(y(t)\) is the (positive) distance that the sand has fallen and \(y(0)=0\) is the surface of the water.
    🔗
    If, as usual, we denote the acceleration due to gravity by \(g=9.8 \frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}^2}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \text{weight of the particle } = gm\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We have assumed that the drag is proportional to velocity so
    \begin{equation*} \text{drag on the particle} = -Rv\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Notice that this force is negative as the drag is necessarily in the direction opposite of the motion, so we assume that the parameter \(R\) is some positive constant and \(v\left(=\dfdx{y}{t}\right)\) is the velocity.
    🔗
    The buoyancy of an object is the upward (negative) force that a surrounding fluid applies to any submerged object. This force is equal to the weight of the fluid being displaced by the object. If we denote the mass of the displaced fluid as \(m_w\) then
    \begin{equation*} \text{buoyancy }=-m_wg\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If the density of the object is less than the density of water then the weight of the object and it’s boyancy will balance before the object is completely covered. That is, any object less dense than water will float atop the water.
    🔗
    Thus equation (19.3) becomes the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} m\dfdx{v}{t}=mg-m_wg-Rv\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we assume that the velocity of the particle at the beginning of its fall is \(v(0)=v_0\) then our model for the velocity of a grain of sand falling through water is the IVP
    \begin{align} m\dfdx{v}{t}\amp{}=mg-m_wg-Rv,\amp{} v(0)=v_0.\tag{19.4} \end{align}
    🔗
    Solving IVP (19.4) will yield a function, \(v(t)\text{,}\) which gives the velocity of the grain as a function of time.
    🔗
    (a)
    The differential equation in IVP (19.4) is actually a separable equation, but this may not be readily apparent. To see this more clearly make the substitution \(M=m-m_w\) and show that the differential equation in IVP (19.4) can be re–expressed as
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{v}}{Rv-Mg}=-\frac{1}{m}\dx{t}\tag{19.5} \end{equation}
    Note that \(M\gt0\) (why?),
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 19.1.4 Deriving the Solution of the Hanging Chain Problem

    We saw in Example 18.2.0.6 that to derive a formula for the catenary curve we need to determine \(z(x)\) from equation (18.19) which requires that we compute the integral \(\int{\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z} } \text{.}\) This will require It is not at all obvious but the substitution we need is \(z=\tan{\theta } \text{.}\) We will discuss how we came up with this particular substitution in the next section. For now we simply observe that this has a slightly different character than those we’ve made before. In the past we made a substitution by setting the substitution variable (usually called \(u\)) equal to some complex expression in order to replace it, thereby making things “easier on our eyes,” or simpler.
    🔗
    But if we make the substitution \(z=\tan(\theta)\) we get \(\dx{\theta{}}=\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\) which results in:
    \begin{equation} \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}= \int\frac{\sec^2(\theta )} {\sqrt{1+\tan^2(\theta)}}\dx{\theta}. \tag{19.19} \end{equation}
    This is not simpler. If anything it is more complicated
     2 
    In this situation a former teacher of one of the authors was fond of joking “Why simplify when you can complicate?”
    . For that reason this form of substitution is sometimes called Inverse Substitution.
    🔗
    But remember that our goal is not to make the integral simpler, our goal is to make the integral doable. In this case the trigonometric identity
    \begin{equation} \sec^2( \theta ) = 1+\tan^2 (\theta )\tag{19.20} \end{equation}
    will be very helpful.
    🔗
    Thus we see that
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{1+\tan^2(\theta )}=\sqrt{\sec^2( \theta )}=\abs{\sec( \theta )} \end{equation*}
    because as we observed in DIGRESSION: The Absolute Value Function \(\sqrt{x^2}=\abs{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The Absolute Value Function is relatively simple but when it appears in conjunction with other more complicated functions it tends to be more difficult to work with than it really should be. It is annoying, but it is also unavoidable. The square root of \(x^2\) is \(\abs{x}\text{,}\)not \(x\) no matter how much we’d like it to be, and that fact has to be dealt with.
    🔗
    In any case dealing with the details of the Absolute Value Function is a complication we don’t need right now so we will impose the condition
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-\pi }{2}\lt x \lt \frac{\pi }{2}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which guarantees that \(\sec \left(\theta \right)\gt 0\) and so \(\abs{\sec (\theta )}=\sec( \theta )\text{.}\) From equation (19.20) we see that
    \begin{align} \int\frac{1} {\sqrt{1+z^2}} \dx{z} \amp{}=\int\frac{\sec^2(\theta)}{\abs{\sec(\theta)}} \dx{\theta}\notag\\ \amp{}=\int\frac{\sec^2(\theta )}{{\sec \left(\theta \right)\ }}\dx{\theta}\notag\\ \amp{}=\int\sec (\theta )\dx{\theta}\notag\\ \int\frac{1} {\sqrt{1+z^2}} \dx{z}\amp{}=\ln \abs{\tan \left(\theta \right) +\sec \left(\theta \right)}+C.\tag{19.21} \end{align}
    Notice the the Absolute Value Function has once again inserted itself into our computations. It will be a bit easier to deal with this time. To finish we need to put everything back in terms of \(z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    From our substitution we have \(\tan(\theta)=z\text{.}\) We could use equation (19.20), to find \(\sec(\theta) \) in terms of \(z\) but it is simpler to recall basic trigonometry, and draw a right triangle with \(\theta \) in standard position as in Figure 19.1.4.1.

    Aside: The Mnemonic Triangle.

    The mnemonic triangle in Figure 19.1.4.1 is slightly deceptive. It assumes that \(\theta \) is in the first quadrant but, as stated, nothing in the problem forces that to be true. In particular \(\theta \) will be in the fourth quadrant if \(z\lt0 \text{.}\) So we may be introducing an error with our sketch. Fortunately the error, if there is one, will only be the algebraic signs of the quantities involved: We might get a negative value when we it should be positive or vice versa. Most of the time this is not an issue — it is not in this problem — and in any case we can correct any such errors after finding the necessary formulas first. This is why we call this a mnemonic and not a technique.

    which we can use to put equation (19.21) in terms of \(z\text{:}\)

    Example 19.1.5.15.

    \begin{equation*} \int\sin^3(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta} \end{equation*}
    Since sine is raised to an odd power we’ll “save one of the sines” and then convert everything else to cosines using the Pythagorean identity
    \begin{equation*} \sin^2(\theta)+\cos^2(\theta)=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    First we “save a sine.”
    \begin{equation*} \int\sin^3(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta} = \int\sin(\theta)\sin^2(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}=\int(1-\cos^2(\theta))\cos^2(\theta)\sin(\theta)\dx{\theta}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Next we let \(z=\cos(\theta)\text{,}\) so that \(\dx{z}=-\sin(\theta)\dx{\theta}\text{,}\) which gives
    \begin{align*} \int\sin^3(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta} \amp = \int(1-z^2)z^2(-\dx{z})\\ \amp =\int(z^4-z^2)\dx{z}\\ \amp =\frac{z^5}{5}=\frac{z^3}{3}+c\\ \amp =\frac{\cos^5(\theta)}{5}=\frac{\cos^3(\theta)}{3}+c\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Had the cosine been raised to an odd power in Example 19.1.5.15, we could have “saved one of the cosines” instead.

    Notice that in Example 19.1.5.15 there would have been nothing wrong with computing \(\int\sin^3(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\) as follows:

    Example 19.1.5.20.

    What happens if you don’t have an odd power of sine or cosine? For example, suppose you have \(\int\sin^2(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Saving a sine or cosine would lead to an integrand with a square root, which we are trying to avoid, if we can help it. In Problem 19.1.5.6 we saw that one of the Half–Angle formulas from Trigonometry was helpful. In fact, the Half-Angle formlas:
    \begin{equation*} \sin^2(\theta) = \frac12\left(1-\cos(2\theta)\right) \text{ and } \cos^2(\theta)=\frac12\left(1+\cos(2\theta)\right) \end{equation*}
    are just the tool we need when there is no way to “save” a sine or cosine. Using these identities in the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int\sin^2( \theta )\cos^2 (\theta )\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    we see that
    \begin{align*} \int\sin^2(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\amp =\frac14\int(1-\cos(2\theta))(1+\cos(2\theta))\dx{\theta}\\ \amp =\frac14\int (1-\cos^2(2\theta))\dx{\theta}\\ \amp =\frac{1}{4}\int\sin^2(2\theta)\dx{\theta} \end{align*}
    which doesn’t seem to help much until we notice that we can use the Half-Angle formula again. This gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{4}\int\sin^2(2\theta)\dx{\theta} = \frac18\int(1-\cos(4\theta))\dx{\theta} = \frac18\left(\theta-\frac14\sin(4\theta)\right)+C \end{equation*}

    🔗
    (a)
    \(\int\sec^4(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\)
    🔗
    We can save a \(\sec^2(\theta)\) as part of the differential and use the identity \(\sec^2(\theta)=1+\tan^2(\theta)\) to change everything else into an expression in tangent. Since the secant is to an even power this again avoids square roots, which is the general idea.
    \begin{align*} \int \sec^4(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\amp{} =\int \sec^2(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\\ \amp{}=\int(1+\tan^2(\theta))\tan^2(\theta)\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta} \end{align*}
    Letting \(y=\tan(\theta)\) we have \(\dx{y}=\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\text{,}\) so we obtain
    \begin{align*} \int(1+y^2)y^2\dx{y}\amp{}=\int(y^2+y^4)\dx{y}\\ \amp{}=\frac{y^3}{3}+\frac{y^5}{5}+C\\ \amp{}=\frac{\tan^3(\theta)}{3}+\frac{\tan^5(\theta)}{5}+C \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    If tangent occurs to an odd power, we can save a tangent and a secant as part of the differential and use the same identity to convert everything else to secant.
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^2(\theta)\tan^3(\theta)\dx{\theta} \amp{}= \int\sec^2(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\sec(\theta)\tan(\theta)\dx{\theta{}}\\ \amp{}=\int\sec^2(\theta)(\sec^2(\theta)-1)\sec(\theta)\tan(\theta)\dx{\theta}. \end{align*}
    Letting \(u=\sec(\theta)\) we have
    \begin{align*} \int u^2(u^2-1)\dx{u}\amp{}=\int (u^4-u^2)\dx{u}\\ \amp{}=\frac{u^5}{5}-\frac{u^3}{3}+C\\ \amp{}=\frac{\sec^5(\theta)}{5}-\frac{\sec^3(\theta)}{3}+C. \end{align*}
