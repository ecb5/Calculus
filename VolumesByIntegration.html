    Section 21.3 Volumes

    The following is a photograph of Problem 14 from the \textit{Moscow Mathematical Papyrus}, an Egyptian mathematical papyrus from around 1850 BC which currently resides in the Pushkin Museum of Fine Arts.
    🔗

    Cornerstone Problem 21.3.0.1.

    The problem is to find the volume of a truncated pyramid (also called a frustum) whose height is \(6\text{,}\) whose base is a square of side \(4\) and whose top is a square with side \(2\text{.}\) The solution translates as follows.
    🔗
    • You are to square \(4\text{,}\) result \(16\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • You are to double \(4\text{,}\) result \(8\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • You are to square \(2\text{,}\) result \(4\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • You are to add the \(16\text{,}\) the \(8\text{,}\) and the \(4\text{,}\) result \(28\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • You are to take a third of \(6\text{,}\) result \(2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • You are to take \(28\) twice, result \(56\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • See it (the volume) is \(56\text{.}\) You will find it right.
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In modern terms, the volume is given by
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3}\cdot 6\cdot \left(4^2+2\cdot 4+2^2\right)=56 \end{equation*}
    which is the correct answer.
    🔗
    Mathematics historians believe that Egyptians, among other early civilizations, knew the general formula for the volume of a truncated pyramid of height \(h\) and square bases whose sides are \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \text{volume}=\frac{1}{3}h\left(a^2+ab+b^2\right) \end{equation*}
    though it would not have been written in such a modern, general form.
    🔗
    🔗
    Mathematics historians are led to believe that Egyptians and other early civilizations also knew the related formula for the volume of a pyramid with height \(h\) and a square base with side \(b\text{,}\) \(volume=\frac{1}{3}hb^2\text{.}\) That being said, historians do not know exactly how either of these formulas were obtained.
    🔗

    Problem 21.3.0.1.

    🔗
    🔗
    Also, one can use the formula for the volume of a pyramid to obtain the formula for the volume of a truncated pyramid as suggested by the Moscow Papyrus. With that in mind, consider the side view of a truncated pyramid with height \(h\) and base sides \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If one were to fill in the top of the pyramid that was chopped off
    🔗
    If the formula for the volume of a pyramid is correct, then the volume of the truncated pyramid would be

    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    There are questions which remain:
    1. Is the formula for the volume of a pyramid correct?
      🔗
    2. Do the same ideas work for other shapes such as a cone or a pyramid with a more general base?
      🔗
    3. What would these formulas look like?
      🔗
    🔗
    Ancient civilizations knew answers to these questions but did not necessarily explain how they were obtained. We can only speculate what they did.
    🔗
    For example, if you use the following cone to fill the corresponding cylinder, it seems that it takes three cones to fill the cylinder.
    🔗
    This would lead one to guess that the volume of a cone is \(\frac{1}{3}\) the height times the area of the base. This coincides with our formula for the volume of a square based pyramid. Could it be that the volume of a cone with an arbitrary shape for the base be \(\frac{1}{3}\) the height times the area of the base as well?
    🔗
    Fortunately, we have integral calculus at our disposal to address these questions. Keeping in mind our general strategy of applying a relatively simple formula on an infinitely small piece and then integrating (adding) these infinitely small quantities, we will need to look at volumes in a more simplistic and general way. Take for example the volume of a rectangular box.
    🔗
    You probably remember the volume of this box as length times width times height. Let’s restate the volume in this way
    \begin{equation*} \text{Volume of a box} = \text{Area of a face} \times \text{Thickness} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    The advantage of thinking this way is that it applies to more general objects. For example, the volume of the following circular cylinder and prism follow the same formula.
    🔗
    This idea even applies to slabs that have an irregular face.
    🔗
    Let’s illustrate this by addressing the volume of cone. Recall that we believe that the volume of a cone is 1/3 the volume of the corresponding cylinder, that is, it is \(\frac{1}{3}H{\pi R}^2\text{.}\) With this in mind, we will put in a vertical axis, label it \(y\text{,}\) and divide the cone into infinitely thin slices. As before, we will only draw one generic slice, but you must imagine that this cone is comprised of infinitely many such slices stacked.
    🔗
    Drawing this generic slice separately we can compute its volume.
    🔗
    Integrating these volumes, we get
    \begin{equation*} \text{ Volume of cone} = \int_{y=0}^R{\pi r^2\dx{y}} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    What is left now is to put \(r\) in terms of \(y\) so the integral can be computed. This can be done by noticing that we have similar triangles.
    🔗
    This gives us the proportion \(\frac{r}{y}=\frac{R}{H}\text{,}\) so \(r=\frac{R}{H}y\text{.}\)
    🔗

    To see how we can obtain the volume of the frustum of a generalized cone, notice that we can write the formulas we’ve already obtained as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3}h\left(a^2+ab+b^2\right)=\frac{1}{3}h\left(a^2+\sqrt{a^2b^2}+b^2\right) \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3}h\left(A+\sqrt{AB}+B\right) \end{equation*}
    where \(h\) is the height and \(A\) and \(B\) are the areas of the two bases.
