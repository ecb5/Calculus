    Section 25.7 Tying Up Some Loose Ends

    So, we have that any power series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{a_n{\left(x-a\right)}^n}\) has a radius of convergence \(r\) where the series converges absolutely for \(|x-a|\lt{}r\) and diverges for \(|x-a|>r\text{.}\) However, as we said before, determining what a series converges to is typically a much harder question than just determining that it converges. For example, the Taylor series representation of a function \(f(x)\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{f^{\left(n\right)}\left(a\right)}{n!}{\left(x-a\right)}^n} \end{equation*}
    will have a radius of convergence, but how do we know that it will actually converge to the function \(f(x)\text{?}\) We know that the geometric series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=1}{x^n}\) converges to \(\frac{1}{1-x}\) for \(\abs{x}\lt{}1\text{,}\) and by virtue of what we did in the last section we know that any series obtained by manipulating this via algebra, differentiation, or integration will converge to the appropriate function. This takes care of representations such as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{nx^{n-1}}=\frac{1}{{\left(1-x\right)}^2}\ \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{{\left(-1\right)}^nx^n}=\frac{1}{1+x}\ \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^n}{n+1}}x^{n+1}={\ln \left(1+x\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{{\left(-1\right)}^n}x^{2n}=\frac{1}{1+x^2}\ \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^n}{2n+1}x^{2n+1}}=\arctan (x) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    which we now know are all valid for \(\abs{x}\lt{}1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    But what about other series? For example, we know that the Taylor Series representations
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{1}{n!}x^n}\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^n}{\left(2n+1\right)!}x^{2n+1}}\ \sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^n}{2n!}x^{2n}} \end{equation*}
    for \(e^x, \sin (x),\cos (x)\) converge for all \(x\text{,}\) but how do we know that they converge to these functions? Let’s go back to how we knew so much about the geometric series. What we know stems from the fact that we had the closed form
    \begin{equation*} 1+x+x^2+\dots +x^N=\frac{1-x^{N+1}}{1-x} \end{equation*}
    which converged to \(\frac{1}{1-x}\) for \(\abs{x}\lt{}1\text{.}\) Written another way, we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1-x}=1+x+x^2+\dots +x^N+\frac{x^{N+1}}{1-x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    so that for a given \(x,\) \(1+x+x^2+\dots +x^N\) converged to \(\frac{1}{1-x}\) exactly when \(\frac{x^{N+1}}{1-x}\) converged to 0. Can we do this for a general Taylor series? The answer is yes, and the key comes from an unlikely candidate -- integration by parts. Specifically, suppose \(f,f',f'',\dots ,f^{\left(N+1\right)}\) are all continuous on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\text{.}\) By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int^x_{t=a}{f'\left(t\right)dt}=f\left(x\right)-f(a) \end{equation*}
    which we will write as
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{1}{0!}\int^x_{t=0}{f'\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^0dt} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    You will see why we wrote this is such a strange way, when we perform integration by parts on the integral. We will make sure not to hide any patterns that will emerge. Let
    \begin{align*} u\amp{}=f'(t) \amp{} dv={\left(x-t\right)}^0dt\\ du\amp{}=f''(t)dt \amp{} v=-\frac{1}{1}{\left(x-t\right)}^1 \end{align*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{1}{0!}\left({\left.(-\frac{1}{1}f'\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^1\right|}^x_{t=a}+\frac{1}{1}\int^x_{t=a}{f''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^1dt}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =f\left(a\right)+\frac{1}{0!}\left(-\frac{1}{1}f'\left(x\right){\left(x-x\right)}^1+\frac{1}{1}f'\left(a\right){\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{1}{1}\int^x_{t=a}{f''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^1dt}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{1}{1!}\int^x_{t=a}{f''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^1dt} \end{equation*}
    Let’s apply integration by parts on this integral.
    \begin{align*} u\amp{}=f''(t) \amp{} dv={\left(x-t\right)}^1dt du\amp{}=f'''(t)dt \amp{} v=-\frac{1}{2}{\left(x-t\right)}^2 \end{align*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{1}{1!}\int^x_{t=a}{f''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^1dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{1}{1!}\left({\left.(-\frac{1}{2}f''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^2\right|}^x_{t=a}+\frac{1}{2}\int^x_{t=a}{f'''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^2dt}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{1}{1!}\left(-\frac{1}{2}f''\left(x\right){\left(x-x\right)}^2+\frac{1}{2}f''\left(a\right){\left(x-a\right)}^2+\frac{1}{2}\int^x_{t=a}{f'''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^2dt}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{f''\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\frac{1}{2!}\int^x_{t=a}{f'''\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^2dt} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Problem 25.7.0.1.

    Perform integration by parts on the above integral to obtain -->
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{f''\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\frac{f'''\left(a\right)}{3!}{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\frac{1}{3!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(4\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^3dt} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    If we continue this, we obtain the following
    🔗

    Theorem 25.7.0.2. Integral Form of the Remainder for Taylor Series.

    Suppose \(f^{\left(N+1\right)}(x)\) is continuous on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\) (which will imply that all the previous derivatives are continuous). Then
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{f''\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\dots +\frac{f^{\left(N\right)}\left(a\right)}{N!}{\left(x-a\right)}^N+\frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    To explain the name of the theorem, we mentioned in Section 7.4 that \(\sum^N_{n=0}{\frac{f^{\left(n\right)}\left(a\right)}{n!}{\left(x-a\right)}^n=}f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{f''\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\dots +\frac{f^{\left(N\right)}\left(a\right)}{N!}{\left(x-a\right)}^N\) is called the \(N^{th}\) degree Taylor polynomial (of \(f\) expanded about \(a\)) and provides an approximation to the function \(f(x)\text{.}\) The quantity \(\frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\) is called the \(N^{th}\) integral form of the remainder, and measures how far off our approximation is. It is clear that the Taylor series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{f^{\left(n\right)}\left(a\right)}{n!}{\left(x-a\right)}^n}\) will converge to \(f(x)\) exactly when
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\right)}=0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To see this in action, we have the following theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.7.0.3.

    If there exists a number \(B\) such that \(\abs{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right)}\le B\) for all nonnegative integers \(N\) and for all \(t\) on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\right)}=0 \end{equation*}
    and so
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{f^{\left(n\right)}\left(a\right)}{n!}{\left(x-a\right)}^n} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Before we show why this is true, recall that we showed that the series \(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{x^n}{n!}}\) converges absolutely for all \(x\text{.}\) This says that \(\limit{N}{ \infty}{ \frac{{\abs{x}}^{N+1}}{(N+1)!}}=0\) for any \(x\text{.}\) We will make use of this fact.
    🔗
    To prove our theorem, we will break up our argument into two cases: \(a\lt{}x\) and \(a>x\text{.}\) The case where \(x=a\) is trivial. For the case where \(a\lt{}x\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0\le \abs{\frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}}\le \frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{\left|f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^N\right|dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \le \frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{B{\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt=B\left(\frac{{\left(x-a\right)}^{N+1}}{\left(N+1\right)!}\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(\limit{N}{ \infty}{ 0=\limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\frac{{\left(x-a\right)}^{N+1}}{\left(N+1\right)!}\right)}}\text{,}\) then by the squeeze theorem,
    \begin{equation*} \limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\frac{1}{N!}\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\right)}=0 \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Before we prove this new theorem, we need to mention a couple of properties of continuous functions that we took as being intuitively obvious before (which in fairness, the entire mathematical community did as well in the 18\({}^{th}\) century). We will state them here formally for reference.
    🔗

    Theorem 25.7.0.8. Extreme Value Theorem.

    If \(f(t)\) is continuous on a closed bounded interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) then it has a global minimum and maximum. That is, there are \(c_0,c_1\in [a,b]\) with
    \begin{equation*} f\left(c_0\right)\le f\left(t\right)\le f\left(c\right) \end{equation*}
    for all \(t\in [a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 25.7.0.9. Intermediate Value Theorem.

    If \(f(t)\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and \(v\) is any value between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\text{,}\) then there is at least one \(c\in [a,b]\) with \(f\left(c\right)=v\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The EVT was stated and utilized without proof in Section 9.5.5 in our discussion of optimization. The IVT was never stated, but we utilized it in Chapter 10 when graphing. Specifically, if we knew that \(f'(t)\neq 0\) on an interval \(I\text{,}\) then either \(f'\left(t\right)>0\) for all \(t\in I\) or \(f'\left(t\right)\lt{}0\) for all \(t\in I\text{.}\) We were assuming that \(f'\) was continuous (though this holds true for derivatives even if they are not continuous). If \(f'(t)\) changed signs on \(I\) then somewhere in between it would need to be 0.
    🔗
    As we said, like the mathematical community in the 18\({}^{th}\) century, we took these to be intuitively obvious and looking a graphs of continuous curves, you could convince yourself that they must be true. It wasn’t until mathematicians began looking into the foundations of calculus that it became evident that more rigorous proofs of these were needed. Even then, the proofs of these required more than just a rigorous definition of continuity. As with many such existence proofs (which establish existence without actually finding the values) these rely on the completeness of the real number system. This is the same axiom that we mentioned about bounded, increasing sequences converging. We mentioned then that there are equivalent formulations of this completeness axiom and there are two versions which are particularly useful in these situations. We will not provide these axioms or proofs of the EVT or IVT, but if you are interested you can find them in \textit{A Contextual Introduction to Real Analysis: How We Got From There to Here }https://ecb5.github.io/SecondEd2/ASORA-2.html or better yet, become a math major and take any standard course in real analysis. They will undoubtedly be proven there.

    Theorem 25.7.0.10. Lagrange’s Form for the Remainder for Taylor Series.

    Suppose \(f\) is a function such that \(f^{\left(N+1\right)}(t)\) is continuous on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{f'\left(a\right)}{1!}{\left(x-a\right)}^1+\frac{f''\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\dots +\frac{f^{\left(N\right)}\left(a\right)}{N!}{\left(x-a\right)}^N+\frac{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(c\right)}{\left(N+1\right)!}{\left(x-a\right)}^{N+1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    where \(c\) is some number (which depends on \(N\)) between \(a\) and \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    You might recognize a special case of this when \(N=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+f'(c)(x-a) \end{equation*}
    where \(c\) is between \(a\) and \(x\text{.}\)

    for some \(c\in [a,x]\text{.}\) With this in mind, since \(f^{\left(N+1\right)}(t)\) is continuous on \([a,x]\) then by the extreme value theorem, it has a minimum \(m\) and a maximum \(M\text{.}\) This says that for all \(t\in [a,x]\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} \int^x_{t=a}{m{\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\le \int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\le \int^x_{t=a}{M{\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} m\frac{{\left(x-a\right)}^{N+1}}{N+1}\le \int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}\le M\frac{{\left(x-a\right)}^{N+1}}{N+1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} m\le \frac{\int^x_{t=a}{f^{\left(N+1\right)}\left(t\right){\left(x-t\right)}^Ndt}}{\frac{{\left(x-a\right)}^{N+1}}{N+1}}\le M \end{equation*}
    🔗

    \begin{align*} 0\amp{}\le \abs{\frac{\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}-1\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}-2\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}-3\right)\dots \left(\frac{1}{2}-N\right){\left(1+x\right)}^{\frac{1}{2}-\left(N+1\right)}}{N!}\frac{{\left(x-c\right)}^N}{{\left(1+c\right)}^{N+\frac{1}{2}}}x}\\ \amp{} =\frac{\frac{1}{2}\left(1-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(2-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(3-\frac{1}{2}\right)\dots \left(N-\frac{1}{2}\right)}{N!}\frac{{\abs{x-c}}^N|x|}{{\left(1+c\right)}^{N+\frac{1}{2}}}\\ \amp{} =\frac{\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)\dots \left(\frac{2N-1}{2}\right)}{N!}\frac{{\left(c-x\right)}^N}{{\left(1+c\right)}^N}\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{1+c}}\\ \amp{}=\frac{1\cdot 1\cdot 3\cdot 5\cdot \dots \cdot \left(2N-1\right)}{2^{N+1}\left(1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot \dots \cdot N\right)}{\left(\frac{c-x}{1+c}\right)}^N\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{1+c}}\\ \amp{}=\frac{1\cdot 1\cdot 3\cdot 5\cdot \dots \cdot \left(2N-1\right)}{2\cdot 2\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdot \dots \cdot 2N}{\left(\frac{c-x}{1+c}\right)}^N\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{1+c}}\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{3}{4}\cdot \frac{5}{6}\cdot \dots \cdot \frac{2N-1}{2N}{\left(\frac{c-x}{1+c}\right)}^N\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{1+c}}\\ \amp{}\le {\left(\frac{c-x}{1+c}\right)}^N\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{1+c}} \end{align*}
    The problem at this point is that \(c\) is not fixed as it depends on \(N\text{.}\) However, we do have \(-1\lt{}x\le c\text{,}\) so that \(0\lt{}1+x\le 1+c\text{.}\) Thus \(0\lt{}\frac{1}{1+c}\le \frac{1}{1+x}\)and \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+c}}\le \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x}}\) . Thus, we have
    🔗
    \begin{align*} 0\amp{}\le \abs{\frac{\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}-1\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}-2\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}-3\right)\dots \left(\frac{1}{2}-N\right){\left(1+x\right)}^{\frac{1}{2}-\left(N+1\right)}}{N!}\cdot{}\frac{{\left(x-c\right)}^N}{{\left(1+c\right)}^{N+\frac{1}{2}}}x}\\ \amp{}\le {\left(\frac{c-x}{1+c}\right)}^N\frac{\left|x\right|}{\sqrt{1+x}} \end{align*}
    🔗
    At this point, \(x\) is fixed so \(\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{1+x}}\) isn’t a problem. However, \(\frac{c-x}{1+c}\) still has \(c\) in it so it is not fixed. We need the following result to close the deal on this.

    Given the answer in the previous problem, it is clear that such a brute force calculation is beyond our capability. However, we know that eventually the harmonic series will surpass 100. Eventually it will even surpass \(1.509\times {10}^{43}\text{.}\) So how does one actually solve
    \begin{equation*} 1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}+\dots +\frac{1}{N}\ge 100 \end{equation*}
    given that it is beyond modern computing capability? Surprisingly, this answer was addressed by Leonard Euler in 1734. Euler showed that
    \begin{equation*} \limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\sum^N_{n=1}{\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)}-{\ln \left(N\right)}\right)} \end{equation*}
    exists. This limit by \(y\) and is referred to as Euler’s constant. Euler described this number as ``worthy of serious consideration’’ and computed its value to six decimal places. Later he computed it to 16 decimal places. For our purposes we will use the approximation \(y\approx .577\text{.}\) To be sure, it is not obvious that this limit even exists and to this day it is not even known if it is irrational. The irrationality of it is one of the major unsolved problems of mathematics today. Before we see why this limit should exist, let’s apply it to our problem of determining approximately how many terms to add to get
    \begin{equation*} 1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}+\dots +\frac{1}{N}\ge 100 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(\limit{N}{ \infty}{ \left(\sum^N_{n=1}{\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)}-{\ln \left(N\right)}\right)}=y\)\textit{} we have that for large value of \(N\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \sum^N_{n=1}{\frac{1}{n}}\approx {\ln \left(N\right)}+.577 \end{equation*}
    So instead of trying to solve
    \begin{equation*} 1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}+\dots +\frac{1}{N}\ge 100 \end{equation*}
    we will solve
    \begin{equation*} {\ln (N)}+.577\ge 100 \end{equation*}
    So that
    \begin{equation*} N\ge e^{99.423} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    One could use an online calculator to determine that this is approximately \(1.509\times {10}^{43}\text{,}\) but in the spirit of Euler, let’s see how we can do this without such advanced computing power. Here is where logarithms shine (and is probably how the calculator was programmed to do it). If we let \(N=e^{99.423}\text{,}\) then we have
    \begin{equation*} \log_{10}( N)=\log_{10} e^{99.423}=99.423\log_{10} e \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Euler would have had access to a table of common logarithms (base 10), and would have known \(\log_{10} e\approx .434294 \text{.}\) Using this we get
    \begin{equation*} \log_{10} N\approx 99.423\times .434294\approx 43.17886 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} N\approx {10}^{43.17886}={10}^{.17886}\times {10}^{43}\approx 1.509\times {10}^{43} \end{equation*}
    🔗
